TSMC sets out manufacturing roadmap to 2029 with new advanced nodes

Blogs
2 mins read

TSMC has outlined its semiconductor manufacturing roadmap through to 2029, signalling how it plans to evolve its process technologies as demand grows for more powerful and energy‑efficient computing, particularly in artificial intelligence and high‑performance computing.

Speaking at its North American Technology Symposium 2026, the company introduced two new sub‑2nm‑class processes, A12 and A13, both now targeted for production in 2029. TSMC also revealed an extension to its 2nm family with the addition of N2U, while confirming that volume production of its A16 process has been pushed back to 2027, one year later than previously expected.

For chip designers, the roadmap indicates a clearer segmentation between cost‑optimised and performance‑driven technologies. TSMC positioned A13 as a key option for next‑generation AI accelerators and high‑end CPUs, while A12 is intended to extend backside power delivery into more advanced data‑centre‑class products. The newly announced N2U process is aimed at providing a more cost‑efficient option across mobile, AI and high‑performance computing applications.

A13 is described as an optical shrink of the forthcoming A14 node and is expected to enter production in 2029. According to TSMC, A13 reduces linear dimensions by about 3 per cent, resulting in an estimated 6 per cent increase in transistor density, while maintaining full electrical and design‑rule compatibility with A14. This approach is designed to allow customers to reuse intellectual property and design assets with minimal additional cost or risk.

TSMC also plans to introduce N2U in 2028 as a lower‑cost upgrade path for designs based on its upcoming N2 process. Built using design‑technology co‑optimisation techniques, N2U is expected to deliver performance gains of around 3 to 4 per cent at the same power, or power savings of 8 to 10 per cent at the same speed. Logic density improvements are expected to be more modest, at around 2 to 3 per cent.

Taken together, N2, N2P, N2U, A14 and A13 are primarily aimed at smartphones and client devices, where power efficiency, cost control and IP compatibility are key considerations. By contrast, A16 and A12 are focused squarely on AI and high‑performance computing workloads, where significantly higher performance and power delivery capability are required.

To address these demands, both A16 and A12 will incorporate TSMC’s Super Power Rail backside power delivery technology, intended to improve power integrity and current delivery in complex, high‑density designs. A16, now expected to reach volume production in 2027, is based on an enhanced N2P design using first‑generation gate‑all‑around nanosheet transistors. TSMC positioned it as a performance‑oriented variant rather than a direct replacement for N2X.

Looking further ahead, A12, scheduled for 2029, is set to represent a more substantial step for TSMC’s data‑centre‑class technologies. It is expected to adopt second‑generation nanosheet GAA transistors alongside the company’s NanoFlex Pro technology, effectively succeeding A16 in the high‑performance segment.

One notable aspect of TSMC’s roadmap is its continued avoidance of High‑NA extreme ultraviolet lithography for these upcoming nodes. Both A13 and A12 are planned to rely on existing EUV technologies, in contrast to Intel’s publicly stated plans to introduce High‑NA EUV from its 14A node onwards, starting around 2027 or 2028.

While this strategy may reduce process complexity and near‑term cost for TSMC and its customers, it could have broader implications for the equipment supply chain. ASML has previously forecast strong demand for its High‑NA lithography systems, with expectations that they would enter large‑scale production in the latter half of the decade and contribute significantly to revenue by 2030.

TSMC’s updated roadmap underlines how the foundry is balancing performance, cost and manufacturing risk while it looks to chart a path beyond 2nm - supporting both mainstream client devices and the rapidly growing AI and data‑centre markets.
