Abstract

Surface terminations critically govern the properties of two-dimensional transition metal carbides and/or nitrides (MXenes), yet a universal strategy to obtain MXenes with uniform and controllable terminations remains elusive. Here we introduce a ‘gas–liquid–solid’ triphasic etching strategy that employs iodine vapour, halide molten salts and MAX phases to produce MXenes with pure and precisely tunable halogen terminations (Cl, Br, I or their combinations). In this process, halide molten salts dissolve iodine via interhalogen anion formation while efficiently transporting etching by-products. The resulting MXenes retain excellent structural integrity, yielding uniformly ordered surfaces. As a representative example, Ti3C2Cl2 shows a 160-fold enhancement in macroscopic conductivity and a 13-fold enhancement in terahertz conductivity relative to conventional Cl/O-terminated Ti3C2, attributed to minimized electron trapping and scattering. Beyond single-halogen terminations, the gas–liquid–solid approach enables dual- and triple-halogen termination control, providing a general platform for tailoring MXene surface chemistry towards advanced (opto)electronic applications.

Main

Two-dimensional transition metal carbides and nitrides, also known as MXenes, have emerged as a large group of multifunctional materials, characterized by the chemical formula Mn+1XnTx (M: transition metal, X: carbon or nitrogen, Tx: surface terminations)1. The Mn+1Xn core of MXenes comprises C/N layers sandwiched between transition metal layers. The partially filled d-orbitals of transition metals endow MXenes with a wealth of free electrons, resulting in outstanding electrical conductivity. The intrinsic metallic nature makes MXenes particularly appealing for applications where efficient charge transport is essential, such as electrocatalysis2, energy storage devices3, electromagnetic interference shielding4, terahertz detectors5 and various (opto)electronic devices. A distinguishing feature of MXenes is their adjustable surface terminations, which cover the exposed metal atoms on the MXene surface. These surface terminations have been shown, both theoretically and experimentally, to determine the intrinsic intralayer charge transport properties of MXenes by manipulating the electronic structure of the surface metal atoms6. In addition, they can markedly impact macroscopic charge transport in MXene films composed of multiple nanoflakes or grains7, with intergrain electron hopping being identified as the dominant mechanism8. However, the currently reported MXenes often possess mixed and mostly randomly distributed terminations, leading to surface disordering that causes severe electron trapping and scattering, consequently compromising the intrinsic charge transport9. Moreover, the lack of MXenes with uniform terminations poses a substantial obstacle to experimentally determining the impact of specific terminations on both intrinsic charge transport and intergrain electron hopping. Achieving this fundamental understanding is crucial for optimizing MXene properties and broadening their range of applications.

The random arrangement of terminations on MXenes stems from the intricate top-down etching synthesis process used to produce these materials from layered ternary metal carbides/nitrides (MAX phases). Typically, the synthesis process involves selectively removing the A-layers (for example, Al or Si) of MAX using strong acidic etchants, which exposes the transition metal atom layers to the surface. Anionic species in the reaction environment tend to bond with the exposed transition metal atoms for charge balance, acting as terminations on the MXene surface. The most common method employed is wet-chemistry etching using F- or Cl-containing aqueous acids, which generally yields MXenes with mixed terminations (–OH, –O or –F/–Cl) arranged randomly10,11. An alternative approach uses Lewis-acid halide salts as etchants in conjunction with molten salts to create a ‘solid–liquid’ reaction environment under inert gas protection12,13,14. This approach also requires subsequent washing steps using aggressive oxidizing agents to eliminate side products, which inadvertently introduces O terminations and causes termination disordering. A few additional etching methods have also been proposed, including electrochemical and hydrothermal etching15,16. However, these approaches similarly result in MXenes characterized by mixed and randomly distributed terminations. Recently, a bottom-up approach was developed to synthesize Ti2CCl2 MXene with uniform terminations17, marking an important step towards MXene synthesis. Nonetheless, this method is currently limited to a few selected types of MXene. It is highly desirable to develop synthesis methods that can be universally applied to diverse MXenes, while ensuring ordered and uniform terminations.

In this work, we develop a ‘gas–liquid–solid’ triphasic etching protocol (denoted GLS) that is capable of synthesizing diverse MXenes characterized by uniform and controllably orchestrated halogen terminations. The etching reaction involves solid-phase MAX precursors, liquid-phase halide molten salt and gas-phase I2 vapour. Iodine is selected as the etching agent because the generated by-products (for example, AlI3 and KI) can be readily removed through simple washing. The effectiveness of iodine as an etchant has been previously verified both in wet-solution phases and in a high-temperature vapour phase18,19,20. The halide molten salt acts as a crucial intermediate that dissolves the I2 etchant by forming interhalogen anions, creates a solid–liquid interface and facilitates the transport of side products for effective etching. After the etching reaction, a simple ethanol wash is able to completely remove generated side products and unreacted reactants, preventing contamination and disordering of the MXene terminations. We showcase the versatility of the GLS method by synthesizing MXenes from eight distinct types of MAX phase. Using Ti3C2 MXene as a representative example, we demonstrate the controlled synthesis of Ti3C2 with uniform Cl, Br and I terminations by utilizing different halide salts. The highly ordered and pure terminations of the yielded MXenes are confirmed by atomic-resolution ultralow-energy secondary-ion mass spectrometry (SIMS) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) characterizations. Importantly, our study highlights the critical role of uniform halogen terminations in substantially improving the charge transport properties of MXenes. For instance, the achieved Ti3C2Cl2 with ordered Cl terminations exhibits a 161.6-fold improvement in macroscopic electrical conductivity and a 12.7-fold enhancement in static terahertz conductivity compared with Ti3C2 with mixed Cl/O terminations (denoted ClO-Ti3C2). These enhancements are attributed to the mitigation of electron trapping and scattering, facilitated by the high structural ordering associated with the uniform Cl terminations. Furthermore, the GLS synthesis demonstrates the controllability to flexibly orchestrate the terminations of MXenes with dual-halogen and triple-halogen terminations. Six examples of Ti3C2 with varying proportions of mixed halogen terminations are successfully synthesized. This tunability opens avenues for further termination modifications with chalcogens or organic groups, as highlighted in previous studies6,21, thereby broadening their functional versatility.

Results and discussion

Synthesis of MXenes with mono-halogen terminations

We started by synthesizing MXenes with mono-halogen terminations using our GLS etching method. Typically, the GLS synthesis was conducted in a sealed glass tube containing iodine, potassium halide salt (KI, KBr or KCl), and MAX as the precursors (Fig. 1a and Supplementary Fig. 1). Upon annealing, the precursors underwent a transformation into a triphasic system, comprising a solid-phase MAX phase, liquid-phase molten potassium halide and gas-phase I2 vapour. The molten potassium halide acted as a crucial reaction intermediate, facilitating the dissolution of I2 vapour by forming interhalogen anions (TI2−, T = I, Br or Cl), which enabled solid–liquid etching and aided in the transport of etchant (TI2−) and side products (AlI3 and KI). During the etching process, halogen anions from the molten salt attached to the exposed metal atoms of MXene, resulting in uniform and impurity-free terminations. Using Ti3AlC2 as a representative example, Fig. 1b illustrates the overall reaction process to obtain Ti3C2 MXenes with uniform halogen terminations (Ti3C2Cl2, Ti3C2Br2 and Ti3C2I2). The overall reaction can be described by equation (1). The side products (AlI3) and unreacted I2 are highly soluble in ethanol, allowing easy removal by washing with ethanol in a glovebox. The generated KI and unreacted potassium halide salts are also soluble in ethanol and can be removed through iterative ethanol washing (Supplementary Fig. 2). The overall synthesis process of MXenes does not involve any hazards or oxidative compounds, effectively avoiding termination contamination. The synthesis yields of Ti3C2Cl2, Ti3C2Br2 and Ti3C2I2 from 1 g of Ti3AlC2 are calculated to be 81%, 83% and 85%, respectively. We demonstrated the synthesis of up to 10 g of Ti3C2Cl2 in a single batch using a custom-designed laboratory set-up, highlighting its strong potential for industrial-scale production (Supplementary Fig. 3). In addition, apart from Ti3AlC2, our GLS approach also works for seven additional MAX phases, including Ti3AlCN, Ti2AlC, Ti2AlN, TiNbAlC, Nb2AlC, Nb4AlC3 and Mo2Ga2C. With an I2–KCl–MAX combination, we exemplified the successful synthesis of Ti3CNCl2 (Supplementary Fig. 4), Ti2CCl2 (Supplementary Fig. 5), Ti2NCl2 (Supplementary Fig. 6), TiNbCCl2 (Supplementary Fig. 7), Nb2CCl2 (Supplementary Fig. 8), Nb4C3Cl2 (Supplementary Fig. 9) and Mo2CCl2 (Supplementary Fig. 10) with uniform halogen terminations.

To validate the synthesis mechanism, we conducted annealing time-dependent ex situ powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD, Cu Kα, λ = 1.54 Å) analysis to track the compound evolution during the synthesis of Ti3C2Cl2 (Supplementary Fig. 11). Given the strong hygroscopic nature of AlI3, all samples underwent rapid ethanol washing before PXRD measurement, during which AlI3, together with I2, can be completely removed. By examining the (0002) peaks of Ti3AlC2 and Ti3C2Cl2, we observed a gradual conversion from Ti3AlC2 to Ti3C2Cl2. After annealing for 54 h, Ti3AlC2 was fully converted to Ti3C2Cl2. In addition, with increasing annealing time, the intensity of characteristic KCl peaks exhibited a continuous weakening trend, while the intensity of characteristic KI peaks gradually increased. This phenomenon indicates the consumption of KCl during the reaction to form Cl terminations on MXenes, generating KI as the side product, thus confirming the reaction mechanism depicted in equation (1).

In addition, the formation of interhalogen anions during the synthesis process was confirmed by the Raman spectroscopy analysis, macroscopic colour changes on the annealing products and density functional theory (DFT) calculations (Supplementary Fig. 12). The presence of interhalogen anions was identified as a crucial factor in promoting etching efficiency. As a control study, PXRD analysis (Supplementary Fig. 13) shows that potassium halide salts alone do not etch the MAX phase to yield MXenes in the GLS system. Moreover, we attempted to synthesize Ti3C2I2 MXene with a solid–gas biphasic system of I2 and Ti3AlC2. After 54 h of annealing, although the formation of Ti3C2I2 was detected in the synchrotron X-ray diffraction (SXRD) pattern, the presence of Ti3AlC2 was still evident, contrasting with the complete conversion of Ti3AlC2 achieved using our triphasic GLS method for the same duration of reaction (Supplementary Fig. 14).

Structural characterizations of MXenes

Like MXenes synthesized through other methods, the obtained Ti3C2Cl2 (Fig. 2a), as well as Ti3C2Br2 and Ti3C2I2 MXenes (Supplementary Fig. 15), display an accordion-like morphology, which is distinguished from the bulk morphology of Ti3AlC2. These layered MXenes can be further delaminated into single- or few-layer nanoflakes using a liquid-phase chemical exfoliation approach with n-butyllithium (Fig. 2b). The Ti3C2Cl2 nanoflakes suspended in N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) present a noticeable Tyndall effect when illuminated by a laser pointer. Meanwhile, the atomic force microscopy (AFM) image discloses the single- or few-layer character of the exfoliated flakes, with the thickness of a single layer measuring approximately 1.3 nm (Supplementary Fig. 16).

Ti K-edge X-ray absorption spectra were next collected to evaluate the chemical and local coordination environments of Ti atoms in the three MXenes. In the X-ray absorption near-edge structure (XANES) spectra (Fig. 2c), all three Ti3C2 MXenes exhibit a similar spectrum shape to their corresponding metal carbide reference, TiC. The absorption edge energy (E0) values were determined to be 4,969.3 eV for Ti3C2Cl2, 4,968.8 eV for Ti3C2Br2 and 4,967.9 eV for Ti3C2I2. The noticeably higher E0 of Ti3C2Cl2 compared with Ti3C2Br2 and Ti3C2I2 suggests stronger hybridization between the surface Ti and Cl orbitals, probably due to the higher electronegativity of Cl atoms. Besides, Ti K-edge Fourier-transformed extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) R-space spectra were derived22 (Supplementary Fig. 17), and wavelet-transform analysis of EXAFS in R and k spaces was further performed for Ti3C2Cl2 (Fig. 2d), Ti3C2Br2 (Fig. 2e) and Ti3C2I2 (Fig. 2f). The contour intensity reveals the coordination environment around Ti atoms, including the first-shell metal-termination scattering (1.67 Å for Ti–Cl, 1.73 Å for Ti–Br and 1.76 Å for Ti–I in R space) and second-shell Ti–C–Ti scattering (2.41 Å for Ti3C2Cl2, 2.55 Å for Ti3C2Br2 and 2.57 Å for Ti3C2I2 in R space). Overall, different halogen terminations cause slight changes in the Ti-termination distance and the Ti3C2 backbone thickness. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) confirmed the presence of mono-halogen terminations in all three MXene samples (Supplementary Fig. 18). No iodine signals were detected in Ti3C2Cl2 and Ti3C2Br2, indicating that the terminations are entirely dependent on the molten salts used in the synthesis. In addition, the Ti 2p and C 1s spectra also indicate the integrity of the Ti3C2 backbone structure (Supplementary Fig. 19). It is important to note that the O 1s signals are attributed to C–O bonds, which may arise from lattice oxygen within the carbon layers, as previously reported6,23.

To elucidate the crystalline structures, SXRD patterns (λ = 0.4142 Å) of the three MXenes were collected, and their atomic structures were determined using DFT calculations (Supplementary Fig. 20). The detailed lattice parameters were derived through Rietveld refinement of the SXRD pattern with the simulated structures24 (Supplementary Table 1). As depicted in Fig. 2g, the SXRD pattern of Ti3C2Cl2 aligns well with a hexagonal structure characterized by the P63/mmc space group, exhibiting lattice parameters of a = b = 3.16 Å and c = 22.19 Å. Similarly, Ti3C2Br2 (a = b = 3.08 Å, c = 24.05 Å; Fig. 2h) and Ti3C2I2 (a = b = 3.09 Å, c = 25.19 Å; Fig. 2i) both exhibit a hexagonal structure. The interlayer distances follow the trend of Ti3C2I2 > Ti3C2Br2 > Ti3C2Cl2, consistent with the atomic size trend of I > Br > Cl.

Highly ordered and uniform terminations

We further sought to reveal the atomic arrangement of the three Ti3C2 MXenes by using quantitative atomic-resolution ultralow-energy SIMS23,25. This technique utilizes low-energy Cs+ ions to achieve layer-by-layer sputtering towards the sample surface at a high incident angle, enabling the elemental distribution to be resolved over the sample with atomic-depth resolution. As expected, the Ti3C2 backbone was distinctly identified in the quantitative SIMS analysis of Ti3C2Cl2 (Fig. 3a), Ti3C2Br2 (Fig. 3b) and Ti3C2I2 (Fig. 3c). O signals were detected only in the C layers, consistent with the previous reports8,23. Notably, on both sides of the Ti3C2 backbone, a monolayer of halogen atoms is visualized as the terminations, with no impurity elements detected between the Ti3C2 layers. Importantly, taking Ti3C2Cl2 as an example, depth profiling across multiple layers further verifies that each termination layer is uniformly Cl-terminated (Supplementary Fig. 21). For comparison, we synthesized ClO-Ti3C2 through the classic Lewis-acid molten salt approach12. By contrast, strong Cl and O signals were observed at the termination positions of ClO-Ti3C2 (Supplementary Fig. 22, and Supplementary Table 2). These findings underscore the uniform nature of the terminations on MXenes synthesized via our GLS method.

In addition, high-resolution TEM images and selected area electron diffraction patterns confirm the excellent crystallinity and hexagonal structure of Ti3C2Cl2, Ti3C2Br2 and Ti3C2I2 (Supplementary Fig. 23). High-angle annular dark-field scanning TEM (HAADF-STEM) was further used to obtain atomic-resolution cross-sectional images of the MXenes. In Ti3C2Cl2 (Fig. 3d), three distinct Ti layers in the Ti3C2 backbone are clearly identified. Due to the relatively low atomic mass, the presence of Cl terminations on the Ti3C2 backbone is barely discernible. By contrast, the heavier atomic masses of Br and I in Ti3C2Br2 (Fig. 3e) and Ti3C2I2 (Fig. 3f) are more clearly visible in the HAADF-STEM images, where they appear as distinct monolayers on both sides of the Ti3C2 backbone. The interlayer distances of Ti3C2Cl2, Ti3C2Br2 and Ti3C2I2 were determined to be 1.12 nm, 1.21 nm and 1.28 nm, respectively, in close agreement with the SXRD analysis. Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) mapping images of Ti3C2Cl2 (Supplementary Fig. 24), Ti3C2Br2 (Supplementary Fig. 25) and Ti3C2I2 (Supplementary Fig. 26) reveal a uniform distribution of Ti, C and halogen elements within individual MXene particles. Quantitative analysis (Supplementary Table 3) indicates that the chemical formulas of the three MXenes can be represented as Ti2.9±0.3C2.0±0.2Cl2.0±0.3O0.1±0.03, Ti3.2±0.3C1.8±0.1Br1.6±0.2O0.1±0.04 and Ti3.3±0.3C1.9±0.1I1.6±0.2O0.1±0.04, closely resembling the ideal Ti3C2Cl2, Ti3C2Br2 and Ti3C2I2 formulas. A small amount of oxygen detected is attributable to lattice oxygen in Ti3C2, consistent with our SIMS analysis. In addition, to systematically evaluate the air-oxidation resistance of MXenes synthesized via our GLS method, we conducted time-dependent XPS and atomic-resolution SIMS on Ti3C2Cl2, Ti3C2Br2 and Ti3C2I2 (Supplementary Figs. 27–29). All samples were obtained from the same batch and stored under ambient conditions for defined periods before testing. The results consistently show that MXenes produced by the GLS method remain stable in air for at least 8 weeks, with no substantial structural changes, particularly in the termination layers.

Charge transport of halogen-terminated MXenes

The highly pure and ordered terminations are expected to minimize electron trapping and scattering in MXenes, potentially leading to a substantial improvement in their charge transport properties. We first assessed the direct-current conductivities σdc of MXene pellets utilizing the temperature-dependent van der Pauw method (Fig. 4a). In general, macroscopic charge transport in MXene-based pellets is dominated by thermally activated intergrain electron hopping, leading to a continuously increasing conductivity as the temperature rises9,26. However, both Ti3C2Cl2 and Ti3C2Br2 exhibit metallic behaviour from 300 K down to ~100 K, with conductivity decreasing as temperature increases. Below 100 K, conductivities of both Ti3C2Cl2 and Ti3C2Br2 rise with increasing temperature. Hall effect measurements on ultrathin Ti3C2Cl2 film further confirmed this phenomenon (Supplementary Fig. 30a,b), which can be attributed to electron localization effects. A similar transition from high-temperature metallic to low-temperature localized electron transport has previously been observed in highly controlled epitaxial thin films of related materials27. Notably, Ti3C2Cl2 exhibited the highest electrical conductivity (51,380 S m−1 at 300 K), compared with Ti3C2Br2 (11,126 S m−1 at 300 K) and Ti3C2I2 (4,149 S m−1 at 300 K). Moreover, we measured the electrical conductivity of previously reported ClO-Ti3C2 (ref. 12) and Ti3C2 MXene synthesized via the conventional wet-chemistry hydrofluoric acid (HF)-etching method10 (denoted HF-Ti3C2) for comparison. Impressively, the Ti3C2Cl2 achieved approximately 160-fold and 13.5-fold increases in macroscopic electrical conductivity relative to ClO-Ti3C2 (318 S m−1 at 300 K) and HF-Ti3C2 (3,815 S m−1 at 300 K), respectively. To better compare the macroscopic electrical conductivity with reported literatures, we fabricated MXene thin films from delaminated MXene flakes and measured their conductivity using the van der Pauw method (Supplementary Fig. 30c). At 300 K, Ti3C2Cl2, Ti3C2Br2 and Ti3C2I2 films exhibited conductivities of approximately 16,000, 3,000 and 800 S cm⁻1, respectively. For comparison, ClO-Ti3C2 and HF-Ti3C2 showed conductivities of approximately 400 and 7,300 S cm⁻1, respectively. These values are comparable with those reported in the literature (Supplementary Table 4). In addition, time-dependent electrical conductivity measurements further demonstrated that Ti3C2Cl2 exhibits superior oxidation resistance compared with ClO-Ti3C2. After 4 weeks of air exposure, the conductivity of the Ti3C2Cl2 film decreased by only 12.5%, whereas that of ClO-Ti3C2 dropped by 86.5% (Supplementary Fig. 31). Both the temperature-dependent behaviour and the relatively high electrical conductivity in macroscopic specimens highlight the excellent chemical and structural quality of the synthesized materials.

We further explored intraflake transport in our MXenes using ultrafast terahertz spectroscopy, leveraging the transient nature of the terahertz electric field to probe short-range charge transport properties at the nanoscale9,28,29. We first conducted frequency-resolved static terahertz conductivity across a ~0.3–2.1 THz range (Supplementary Fig. 32), with the average terahertz conductivity values derived and compared in Fig. 4b. Ti3C2Cl2 exhibits the highest average terahertz conductivity (5,056 S m−1), surpassing that of Ti3C2Br2 (3,230 S m−1) and Ti3C2I2 (1,551 S m−1) (Methods). Compared with ClO-Ti3C2 (397 S m−1), Ti3C2Cl2 achieved a 13-fold increase in terahertz conductivity, highlighting a substantial enhancement in its intrinsic intraflake conductivity. Considering the 160-fold improvement in macroscopic electrical conductivity, we conclude that the uniform Cl terminations in Ti3C2Cl2 also substantially enhance intragrain electron hopping compared with ClO-Ti3C2.

Optical pump–terahertz probe (OPTP) spectroscopy elucidated the charge carrier dynamics and mechanisms governing the intrinsic intraflake charge transport of MXenes. In the OPTP measurement, additional charge carriers were photogenerated in MXenes using an optical pump pulse (1.55 eV laser pulse with ~50 fs duration) and then probed by a single-cycle terahertz pulse. The relative attenuation of the terahertz field (−ΔE/E) is directly linked to the terahertz photoconductivity (∆σTHz). Figure 4c illustrates the photoconductivity dynamics of our three MXenes in comparison with ClO-Ti3C2. In each sample, a transient increase in photoconductivity signifies the generation of free carriers, followed by rapid decay due to recombination or trapping effects9. Moreover, ∆σTHz is correlated with the charge carrier mobility (µ) as described by equation (2), where N, e, ϕ and Nabs represent the charge carrier density, elementary charge, photon-to-free-charge conversion quantum yield and absorbed photon density, respectively. Assuming a similar ϕ for all MXene samples, the highest photoconductivity per absorbed photon density (∆σTHz/Nabs) observed in Ti3C2Cl2 (9.3 × 10−18 S m) indicates its largest charge carrier mobility, while that of ClO-Ti3C2 is 2.6 × 10−18 S m. Quantitatively, Ti3C2Cl2 demonstrates a 3.6-fold enhancement in charge carrier mobility compared with ClO-Ti3C2.

DFT calculations were used to simulate the band structures of Ti3C2 MXenes, providing deeper insights into how Cl terminations enhance charge transport properties. We noticed that different halogen terminations substantially alter the band structure of Ti3C2 MXene, with Ti3C2Cl2 (Fig. 4d) exhibiting the highest electron density around the Fermi level compared with Ti3C2Br2 and Ti3C2I2 (Supplementary Fig. 33). This effect is probably due to the higher electronegativity of Cl atoms, which enhances hybridization between the surface Ti and halogen orbitals. We also simulated the band structure of ClO-Ti3C2 (with a Cl/O ratio of 0.6/1.4; Supplementary Fig. 34), which presents minimal differences compared with Ti3C2Cl2. To further assess the impact of termination purity, electron transport through Ti3C2Cl2 and ClO-Ti3C2 lattices was simulated using the non-equilibrium Green’s function method in a two-probe pattern. Figure 4e and Fig. 4f visualize the electron transmission pathways within the Ti3C2Cl2 and ClO-Ti3C2 lattices, respectively. Clearly, Ti3C2Cl2 demonstrates superior electron delocalization across the probes, whereas ClO-Ti3C2 shows notable electron localization near the left probe, leading to strong electron scattering. This interpretation is further supported by the quantified current–voltage curves (Fig. 4g) and electron transmittance patterns (Supplementary Fig. 35) of Ti3C2Cl2 and ClO-Ti3C2. Together, these experimental and theoretical findings emphasize the critical role of uniform Cl terminations in enhancing the charge transport capabilities of MXenes.

Electromagnetic wave (EMW) absorption measurements were carried out to evaluate the practical applicability of the halogen-terminated samples30. As shown in Supplementary Fig. 36a, Ti3C2Cl2 exhibits strong EMW absorption in a frequency range of ~14–18 GHz, with a minimum reflection loss (RLmin) of −51.4 dB and a maximum effective absorption bandwidth (EABmax) of 4.6 GHz. Meanwhile, Ti3C2Br2 exhibits strong absorption in the ~12–17 GHz range, with an RLmin of −54.6 dB and an EABmax of 4.6 GHz (Supplementary Fig. 36b). In comparison, Ti3C2I2 shows weaker EMW absorption, mainly in the ~16–18 GHz range, with an RLmin of −27.5 dB and an EABmax of 4.7 GHz (Supplementary Fig. 36c). This RLmin value is much higher than the previously reported Ti3C2Tx (~−20 dB) prepared by wet-chemistry methods31,32. By contrast, ClO-Ti3C2 shows its primary absorption at lower frequencies of ~6–9 GHz, with a RLmin of −61.4 dB and an EABmax of 4.4 GHz (Supplementary Fig. 36d). These results demonstrate that tuning the surface terminations can effectively shift the primary EMW absorption frequency range of Ti3C2 MXenes. This result suggests a promising strategy for designing MXene-based absorbers tailored to specific frequency bands for various practical applications.

Controllable orchestration of halogen terminations

The strong dependence of MXene charge transport properties on termination type further motivated us to pursue controllable orchestration of halogen terminations on MXenes through our GLS synthesis method. In particular, DFT calculations not only revealed that mixed-halogen terminations are thermodynamically stable, but also demonstrated that they possess a more stable structure compared with single-halogen terminations (Supplementary Fig. 37). We noticed that the termination type of MXenes is directly determined by the chosen molten salt in the GLS synthesis. While mono-type potassium halide salts were initially used to achieve single-halogen terminations, we further employed mixed molten salts to synthesize MXenes with dual- and triple-halogen terminations. Specifically, we synthesized Ti3C2 MXene with mixed halogen terminations: I:Br = 1:1 (Fig. 5a), I:Cl = 1:1 (Fig. 5b), Br:Cl = 1:1 (Fig. 5c) and I:Br:Cl = 1:1:1 (Fig. 5d). In addition, taking Ti3C2 with mixed I/Br terminations as a model, we demonstrated precise control over the I/Br termination ratio with I:Br = 1:1 (Fig. 5a), I:Br = 1:2 (Fig. 5e) and I:Br = 2:1 (Fig. 5f). These examples showcase the capability of our GLS method in controllably orchestrating halogen terminations, which would further expand the MXene family and offer a venue to precisely customize the intrinsic properties of MXenes. Finally, Supplementary Table 5 compares the GLS method with previously reported MXene synthesis approaches, underscoring its unique advantages in termination chemistry, structural stability, production scalability and electrical conductivity.

Conclusion

In summary, we have demonstrated a triphasic GLS etching method that is applicable to eight different types of MAX phase, enabling the production of their corresponding MXenes with uniform halogen terminations. This process integrates solid-phase MAX precursors, liquid-phase halide molten salts and gas-phase I2 vapour. The resulting MXenes are characterized by highly pure and ordered halogen terminations (Cl, Br or I). As a representative case, Ti3C2Cl2 synthesized via this method exhibited a 160-fold improvement in macroscopic charge transport, a 13-fold enhancement in static terahertz conductivity and a 3.6-fold rise in charge carrier mobility, compared with ClO-Ti3C2. These enhancements are attributed to the uniform Cl terminations with superior structural ordering, which effectively reduces electron trapping and scattering. In addition, the GLS synthesis demonstrated the capability for controllable orchestration of dual- and triple-halogen terminations on MXenes. Beyond broadening the MXene family with finely adjustable properties, controlled halogen terminations offer the potential for further surface functionalization, such as selective postsubstitution or covalent grafting on specific halogen terminations6,21. We envision that our GLS method will accelerate the development of MXenes with customized properties for advanced (opto)electronics and beyond.

Methods

Chemicals

Ti3AlC2 and Nb2AlC MAX phases were purchased from Carbon-Ukraine. Ti3AlCN, Ti2AlC, TiNbAlC, Mo2Ga2C, Ti2AlN and Nb4AlC3 MAX phases were purchased from Jilin 11 Technology. Elemental iodine (I2), 2.5 M n-butyllithium solution in hexane, N-methylformamide (NMF), ethyl acetate (EA), HNO3, cellulose and potassium chloride (KCl) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. Hexane, tetrahydrofuran, potassium iodide (KI) and potassium bromide (KBr) were purchased from Alfa Aesar. High-purity ethanol (absolute, analytical reagent grade) was bought from Fisher Chemical. All chemicals were used directly without any purification.

Synthesis of MXenes by the GLS method

The GLS method used MAX phase, elemental iodine and halide salts to create a triphasic system for MXene synthesis. Taking the synthesis of Ti3C2Cl2 as an example, typically, 500 mg Ti3AlC2, 1,956 mg I2 and 575 mg KCl were mixed (molar ratio of 1:3:3) and placed in a home-made glass tube (Supplementary Fig. 1). The tube was connected to a Schlenk line, purged with Ar gas three times to completely remove air and then vacuum-sealed to 10−2 mbar. The tube was flame-sealed with a butane torch (model: CHF CF211). Due to the volatility of I2, precursor materials were kept at the bottom of the glass tube in an ice-water bath during sealing to prevent iodine vapourization. The sealed glass tube was calcined at 600 °C in a muffle furnace at a heating rate of 3 °C min−1 for 54 h. After the reaction, the product was removed from the glass tube and washed eight times with high-purity ethanol to remove by-products such as AlI3 and KI, as well as unreacted KCl and I2. Each washing step involved 10 min of sonication in an ice-water bath to assist in dissolving any remaining salts. These washing steps were conducted in an Ar-filled glovebox to prevent MXene oxidation. Finally, the product was vacuum-dried at room temperature for 24 h to obtain Ti3C2Cl2.

The synthesis of Ti3CNCl2, Ti2CCl2, Ti2NCl2, TiNbCCl2, Nb2CCl2, Nb4C3Cl2 and Mo2CCl2 was conducted using the same methodology. The synthesis process of Ti3C2Br2 and Ti3C2I2 follows a similar procedure, differing only in the choice of molten salt: the Ti3AlC2–I2–KBr combination for Ti3C2Br2 and the Ti3AlC2–I2–KI combination for Ti3C2I2. For MXenes with dual- and triple-halogen terminations, precursor ratios were adjusted as follows: Ti3AlC2–I2–KI–KBr (1:3:2.5:0.5) for I:Br = 1:1, Ti3AlC2–I2–KI–KCl (1:3:2.9:0.1) for I:Cl = 1:1, Ti3AlC2–I2–KBr–KCl (1:3:2.5:0.5) for Br:Cl = 1:1, Ti3AlC2–I2–KI–KBr–KCl (1:3:2.8:0.1:0.1) for I:Br:Cl = 1:1:1, Ti3AlC2–I2–KI–KBr (1:3:1.5:1.5) for I:Br = 1:2 and Ti3AlC2–I2–KI–KBr (1:3:2.9:0.1) for I:Br = 2:1. Ti3C2I2-GS was synthesized following the same method as Ti3C2I2 but without the inclusion of any halide salts. The synthesis of ClO-Ti3C2 was conducted according to a previously reported procedure12.

The delamination process of MXenes was carried out using an intercalation method8. Initially, 50 mg of MXenes and 1 ml of 2.5 M n-butyllithium solution were mixed in an Ar-filled glovebox and stirred at room temperature for 24 h. Following this, the material underwent washing steps with high-purity hexane and tetrahydrofuran. The washed sample was then transferred to a centrifuge tube containing 10 ml of anhydrous NMF and sonicated in an ice-water bath (<5 °C) for 40 min. The solution was centrifuged at 360g for 20 min, and the supernatant was collected. The resulting supernatant was further centrifuged at 13,000g for 15 min to isolate MXene nanoflakes, which were then dispersed in anhydrous NMP for subsequent use.

Characterizations

XPS was analysed by an Axis Supra spectrometer (Kratos Analytical). PXRD was performed by the AERIS XRD system (PANalytical) using Cu Kα. SEM images were collected using Zeiss Gemini S4 500. AFM images were collected using Park NX10 AFM. TEM investigations were conducted using a Talos F200X transmission microscope at 200 kV. The measurements were carried out in a STEM mode using the high-angle annular dark-field detector. EDX was used for chemical composition analysis. SIMS was performed via CAMECA IMS SC Ultra instrument with a Cs+ primary-ion beam23. The primary ion beam featured an ultralow impact energy of 100 eV, a moderate current of 6 nA and a standard spot diameter of 30 µm. Measurements were conducted using a negative detector polarity. Initially, a parallel beam of primary Cs+ ions was applied to gently remove surface impurities and promote the formation of a flat, clean crystal surface. Subsequently, low-energy Cs+ ions were directed towards the sample at a high incident angle to ensure selective interaction with only the topmost atomic layer. During this bombardment, atoms from the sample surface were sputtered off and detected as secondary ions.

Synchrotron XAS measurements were conducted at the P65 beamline of the PETRA III synchrotron (DESY, Hamburg, Germany). The XAS spectra were acquired in transmission mode at room temperature with photon energies centred around the Ti K edge (4,966 eV). The incident, transmitted and reference X-ray intensities were recorded using gas ionization chambers. A titanium foil standard was simultaneously measured with the samples and used for energy calibration. Before measurements, all powder samples were pressed into 8-mm pellets, homogeneously mixed with an appropriate amount of cellulose, and sealed between Kapton tapes inside an Ar-filled glovebox. The collected XANES and EXAFS data were subsequently processed and corrected using the Athena software (version 0.9.26)22. The wavelet transform analysis of the EXAFS spectra was carried out using the HAMA Fortran software. The EXAFS oscillation function, χ(k) (k-weights: 2), was extracted as a function of the photoelectron wave number k, within the selected radial range of 0–6 Å (Rmin = 0, Rmax = 6). The Morlet function was chosen as the mother wavelet, with parameters set to kappaMorlet = 10 and sigmaMorlet = 1. SXRD measurements were performed at the BL04 beamline of the ALBA synchrotron facility using an incident energy of 35 keV (λ = 0.4142 Å) and a high-angular-resolution multicrystal analyser detector set-up. The MXene powders were loaded into 0.3-mm-diameter glass capillaries (Hilgenberg GmbH) and mounted on the dedicated sample stage provided by the beamline. The collected SXRD data were analysed using the GSAS-II software, and Rietveld refinement was used to determine the lattice parameters24.

Conductivity measurements

The macroscopic direct current temperature-dependent electrical conductivity of the MXene pellets was measured using the van der Pauw method in a commercial Physical Property Measurement System cryostat equipped with the electrical transport option and automatic contact switching (Quantum Design). Ohmic contacts were established using gold-plated spring-loaded probes on the clean surfaces of the MXene pellets. MXene pellets were made by using compressed powders with a diameter of 5 mm and a thickness of less than approximately 1 mm. The sheet resistance (Rs) of the pellet in the temperature range of ~5–300 K was determined to calculate the bulk electrical resistivity (ρ) and electrical conductivity (σdc). MXene ultrathin films used for the conductivity measurement were prepared as follows. First, exfoliated MXenes were dispersed in NMF to form a stable suspension (~1 mg ml−1). A volume of 1.5 ml of this suspension was then added to 40 ml of deionized water in a 60-ml wide-mouth beaker, followed by the addition of 1 ml of 1 M HNO3 to stabilize the surface potential. After thorough mixing, 2 ml of EA was introduced, and the beaker was covered to approximately 90% of its opening, allowing the EA to evaporate slowly. Once the EA had completely evaporated, the MXene nanosheets spontaneously self-assembled into a continuous single-layer ultrathin film at the water-air interface. These films were subsequently transferred onto gold prepatterned four-probe SiO2/Si substrates and dried under vacuum. To minimize cracks or discontinuities, two layers of film were transferred for each sample.

Terahertz spectroscopy

Terahertz spectroscopy was conducted using a commercial regenerative amplified mode-locked Ti:sapphire femtosecond laser system (Spectra Physics Spitfire Ace, seeded by Mai Tai and pumped by Empower). The laser produced ~50-fs pulses centred at 800 nm with a repetition rate of 1 kHz. Terahertz radiation was generated via optical rectification in a nonlinear zinc telluride (ZnTe) crystal oriented along the (110) direction. The transmitted THz electric field was detected using a second ZnTe crystal through the electro-optic sampling technique, probed by an 800-nm gating pulse. This configuration allowed frequency-resolved measurements in the range of 0.2−2.5 THz.

To determine the terahertz conductivity of MXene thin films without photoexcitation, the transmitted terahertz pulse through the sample (Esample) was compared with that through a reference substrate (Ereference). When the film thickness (d) is much smaller than the terahertz wavelength (d/λ << 1), the thin-film approximation can be applied, as illustrated in equation (3). Here, \(T\,(=\frac{{E}_{\mathrm{sample}}}{{E}_{\mathrm{reference}}})\) denotes the transmission ratio between the sample and reference signals, ∆σTHz represents the complex terahertz conductivity, Z0 = 377 Ω is the impedance of free space and nsub = 1.95 corresponds to the refractive index of the fused-silica substrate in the terahertz range.

$${\Delta \sigma }_{\mathrm{THz}}=\frac{1+{n}_{\mathrm{sub}}}{{Z}_{0}d}\left(\frac{1}{T}-1\right)$$
(3)

For OPTP measurements, the terahertz absorption induced by photoexcited charge carriers (excitation wavelength 800 nm) was monitored by fixing the sampling beam at the peak of the terahertz electric field. Time-dependent, pump-induced terahertz absorption was recorded by varying the temporal delay between the pump and terahertz probe pulses. To eliminate interference from water vapour absorption, the entire terahertz set-up was continuously purged with dry nitrogen. During measurements, the samples were either maintained under a dry nitrogen atmosphere or kept under vacuum (<2 × 10−4 mbar). The time-resolved photoconductive response (∆σTHz) of charge carriers was determined from the time-dependent change in the transmitted terahertz field (∆E) using the thin-film approximation, as expressed by equation (4), where Epump and E0 correspond to the peak amplitudes of the transmitted terahertz field with and without optical excitation, respectively.

$${\Delta \sigma }_{\mathrm{THz}}=\frac{-{n}_{\mathrm{sub}}+1}{{Z}_{0}l}\times \frac{{E}_{\mathrm{pump}}-{E}_{0}}{{E}_{0}}$$
(4)

EMW absorption measurement

Similar to previously reported methods30, all samples were mixed with paraffin and fabricated into concentric rings with an inner diameter of 3.04 mm and an outer diameter of 7 mm for testing. The measurements were carried out using a standard coaxial-line method, using a vector network analyser (N5222B) to record the electromagnetic parameters, including the complex permittivity (εr = ε′ – jε′) and permeability (μr = μ;′ – jμ′).

First-principles calculations

Spin-polarized DFT calculations were carried out using Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP)33,34, based on the plane-wave projector augmented-wave method. The exchange-correlation functional was used in the generalized gradient approximation of Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof35. Van der Waals interactions were taken into account by using the Grimme (DFT-D3) method36. An energy cut-off of 500 eV was set for all the simulations. The Brillouin zone of the systems was sampled using 5 × 5 × 1 Monkhorst−Pack k-points37. The VASPKIT code was used for postprocessing of the VASP calculated data38. The electron transport was calculated within the non-equilibrium Green’s function technique as implemented in the Atomistix ToolKit package (QuantumWise A/S. Atomistix ToolKit, Version 2016.4). We used the Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof functional with single-zeta plus polarization numerical basis sets for all the transport simulations. The kinetic energy cut-off of 75 Ryd was set for the self-consistent calculations. A fast Fourier transform solver was used to address the system’s Poisson–Schrödinger equation in a self-consistent manner. The transport models were treated as two-probe systems with the central scattering region sandwiched between the semi-infinite source (left) and drain (right) electrodes from the same. The current through the scattering region is calculated within the Landauer formula (equation (5)), where \({T}\left(\varepsilon ,{V}\right)\) represents the energy- and voltage-resolved transmission function, f is the Fermi–Dirac distribution function and \({\mu }_{{\rm{L}},{\rm{R}}}={E}_{{\rm{f}}}\pm eV/2\) stands for the chemical potentials of the left and right electrodes. The transport calculations were performed at T = 300 K.
