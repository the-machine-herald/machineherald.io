Abstract

High-risk smoldering multiple myeloma (HR-SMM) carries an increased risk of progression to multiple myeloma, making it an ideal setting to test whether chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cell therapy can achieve curative outcomes. Here in this phase 2 study, patients with HR-SMM received ciltacabtagene autoleucel (cilta-cel) at 0.3–0.5 × 106 or >0.5 × 106 viable CAR+ T cells per kilogram without induction or bridging therapy. Patients with >40% marrow involvement were excluded. Primary endpoints were dose-limiting toxicities (DLTs) and treatment-emergent adverse events; secondary endpoints included response and minimal residual disease (MRD) negativity. As of 11 February 2026, 20 patients had been treated. The trial met the prespecified endpoints. No DLTs occurred. Adverse events included transient cytopenias (90% grade 3/4) and cytokine release syndrome (100% grade 1/2). Non-immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome neurologic toxicities (NINTs) occurred in seven patients, with four comprising cranial nerve palsies that completely resolved. Three patients had persistent grade 1 symptoms. At a median follow-up of 15.3 months, all patients achieved MRD negativity 10−6 by 2 months and have remained MRD negative. Sixteen patients with follow-up >6 months achieved a complete response; no progression or deaths were observed. Cilta-cel produced rapid, deep, sustained MRD-negative responses in HR-SMM without induction therapy. Toxicities were consistent with the safety profile of cilta-cel. ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT05767359.

Main

Ciltacabtagene autoleucel (cilta cel), a B cell maturation antigen (BCMA)-directed CAR T cell therapy, is approved for patients with relapsed or refractory multiple myeloma (RRMM) who have received one prior line of therapy and are lenalidomide refractory1. Although cilta-cel can produce durable responses beyond 5 years in a subset of heavily pretreated patients with RRMM2, most patients ultimately have disease progression, with a median progression-free survival (PFS) of approximately 34 months in late-line disease3.

Clinical data suggest that efficacy may be greater when CAR T cell therapy is administered earlier in the disease course than in later lines, potentially reflecting greater T cell fitness and a less immunosuppressive host environment4. These observations have generated interest in evaluating CAR T cell therapy in earlier disease states, and ongoing trials are assessing cilta-cel in newly diagnosed multiple myeloma5,6.

HR-SMM is associated with a substantial risk of progression to symptomatic multiple myeloma with end-organ damage7,8,9,10,11,12,13. Genomic studies indicate that HR-SMM is often a biologically mature malignancy that resembles overt myeloma although with less genetic heterogeneity, lower burden of disease and, in many cases, better-preserved immune function14,15,16.

Over the past decade, multiple early-intervention strategies have been tested in HR-SMM with the goals of delaying progression and, more recently, achieving deep remissions that may intercept disease evolution17,18. Single-agent daratumumab is the first therapy approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for HR-SMM19. However, multiagent regimens adapted from symptomatic myeloma (triplets and quadruplets) have achieved MRD negativity in only approximately half of patients, suggesting that alternative approaches may be required to achieve durable, treatment-free remissions in this precursor setting.

We hypothesized that cellular therapy could produce deep and durable responses in HR-SMM because of lower tumor burden, reduced genomic complexity and higher T cell fitness, thereby enhancing the benefit from T-cell-redirecting therapies and raising the possibility of cure20. We, therefore, conducted a phase 2 trial to evaluate the efficacy and safety of cilta-cel in patients with HR-SMM and to explore biomarkers associated with response and toxicity.

Results

Patient disposition and baseline demographics

Between 15 April 2023 and 3 July 2025, 23 patients were enrolled; 20 underwent leukapheresis and received a single cilta-cel infusion (Fig. 1a). Planned dosing included an initial safety run-in (six patients; standard 3 + 3 design) to evaluate escalating dose levels (0.5 × 106 and 0.5–1.0 × 106 viable CAR+ T cells per kilogram), followed by dose expansion at the protocol-specified target dose (0.5–1.0 × 106 viable CAR+ T cells per kilogram) (Fig. 1b). Due to emerging data that peak absolute lymphocyte count (ALC) may be associated with increased risk of neurologic toxicities, along with low threshold for non-ICANS neurologic toxicities (NINTs) in this population, the protocol was amended and the final seven patients received 0.3 × 106 viable CAR+ T cells per kilogram. All patients had successful manufacturing of cilta-cel, and no patient discontinued the study prior to cilta-cel infusion. At the data cutoff for this report (11 February 2026), all 20 treated patients remained in the primary analysis population.

Among treated patients, the median age was 58 years (range, 37–78), and the median time between initial diagnosis and enrollment was 16.1 months (interquartile range (IQR), 8.4–36.2) (Table 1). Across the cohort, the median bone marrow plasma cell infiltration was 20% (IQR, 20–30), consistent with the protocol-defined eligibility criterion limiting marrow involvement to 40% or less. Thirteen patients (65%) harbored high-risk cytogenetic abnormalities, defined in this study as del(17p), t(4;14), t(14;16), del(13q) or gain or amplification of chromosome 1q. M-protein levels, serum free light chain ratios and other tumor surrogate markers were within ranges typical for SMM. Thirteen patients (65%) met the 20–2–20 definition of high risk (Supplementary Table 1).

The median time from screening to leukapheresis was 15 days (IQR, 12–27), and the median time from leukapheresis to CAR T cell infusion was 56 days (IQR, 52–76), reflecting a protocol-specified 1-month stagger implemented for the initial cohort. Bridging therapy before cilta-cel infusion was not permitted per protocol. According to established risk stratification tools developed in the relapsed myeloma CAR T cell setting, including CAR-HEMATOTOX and MyCARE, all patients in this cohort were at low risk for survival or toxicity events21,22.

Primary outcome and safety analysis

The primary endpoint of this study was safety. Adverse events were reported in all 20 treated patients (Table 2 and Supplementary Table 2). No patients met protocol-defined DLT criteria in the safety run-in cohort.

Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) occurred in all patients, with 17 (85%) cases of grade 1, three (15%) cases of grade 2 and no grade 3 or higher CRS events. Tocilizumab was administered in 17 patients (85%) and dexamethasone in 13 patients (65%) using a low clinical threshold.

Neutropenia was common, with grade 4 observed in 11 (55%) patients and grade 3 observed in seven (35%) additional patients. These events were predominantly short-lived, with a median duration of 7 days (IQR, 4−13) (Extended Data Fig. 1a). Accordingly, all patients were classified as low risk by immune effector cell-associated hematotoxicity (ICAHT) grading (Extended Data Fig. 1b)23. Severe thrombocytopenia or anemia (grade 3 or 4) was uncommon, occurring in three and two patients, respectively (Extended Data Fig. 1c,d). Two cases of severe thrombocytopenia had features consistent with immune thrombocytopenia; one of these patients also developed hemolytic anemia, possibly related to fludarabine. These events were transient and responded to corticosteroids and intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG). No persistent hematologic toxicity was observed.

Infectious adverse events were all grade 1−2, primarily affecting the upper respiratory tract (25%). All patients received IVIG prophylaxis.

Seven patients developed NINTs, four comprising cranial nerve palsies (three grade 1 and one grade 2), with objective improvement after 2 weeks and full resolution within 4−8 weeks (Table 3). Five of seven patients with NINTs received 0.5−0.8 × 106 CAR+ T cells per kilogram dose. Median time to onset was 21 days (range, 10−52). The only grade 2 event included bilateral cranial nerve VII palsies with mononeuritis multiplex in a patient with possible predisposing risk factor of diabetes mellitus and was treated with dexamethasone and IVIG, resulting in complete resolution. Grading of NINTs was based on symptom severity, and management was deliberately proactive and, in some cases, escalated out of caution rather than due to higher-grade neurologic deficits.

Three patients had other ongoing NINTs (non-cranial nerve palsies) at the data cutoff after doses of 0.8 × 106, 0.7 × 106 and 0.3 × 106 viable CAR+ T cells per kilogram (Extended Data Fig. 2). Patients 14 and 18, both treated at ≥0.5 × 106 viable CAR+ T cell dose, had grade 1 movement and neurocognitive symptoms, including one of the following: tremor, bradykinesia or bradyphrenia, gait disturbance or postural instability, dysarthria or cognitive or personality changes. Patient 18 had deposition of phosphorylated α-synuclein on skin biopsy, suggesting a potential underlying predisposition to Parkinson’s disease24. Despite these symptoms, both patients remained fully functional, did not require assistive devices and continued to work with minimal interference with daily activities. Patient 25, treated at 0.3 × 106 viable CAR+ T cell dose, had grade 1 intention tremor during fine motor tasks without interference with daily living activities. Longitudinal CAR T cell measurements in peripheral blood and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) demonstrated heterogeneous kinetics: patient 18 had persistent CAR T cell detection in peripheral blood and CSF through month 15 (predominantly CD4 CAR+ T cells); patient 14 cleared CAR T cells from peripheral blood by day 56 with no detection in CSF; and patient 25 had CAR T cells detectable in peripheral blood through day 100 with limited CSF assessment (day 52 cytology negative; flow cytometry not performed). Brain magnetic resonance imaging was normal for patients 18 and 14. Therapeutic approaches varied and included high-dose corticosteroids and IVIG (all three), symptomatic agents (amantadine and/or dopaminergic therapy) and, in patient 18, escalation to intrathecal chemotherapy, low-dose ruxolitinib and cyclophosphamide. None of the patients developed symptoms exceeding grade 1 per Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE). The initiation of interventions was not prompted by worsening neurologic toxicity but, rather, reflected a preemptive management strategy to prevent further symptom progression. Clinical responses were mixed: tremor resolution and improvement in bradykinesia and bradyphrenia in patient 14; improved but persistent symptoms in patient 18; and improvement in intention tremor in patient 25.

No immune effector cell-associated hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis-like syndrome (IEC-HS) syndrome was observed. One patient developed transient grade 3 aspartate aminotransferase (AST) and alanine aminotransferase (ALT) elevation after cilta-cel infusion in the setting of recent grade 1 CRS, with concurrent grade 3 hypertriglyceridemia, and improved after dexamethasone. A second patient had transient grade 3–4 hypertriglyceridemia after grade 1 CRS without concurrent clinically significant transaminitis or other features suggestive of IEC-HS.

Gastrointestinal adverse events were limited to transient grade 1–2 diarrhea and vomiting. These events were temporally associated with lymphodepleting chemotherapy, cilta-cel infusion and/or low-grade CRS, and no cases were consistent with characteristics of immune effector cell-associated enterocolitis (IEC-enterocolitis).

Secondary outcomes: response and efficacy assessment

The secondary endpoints of this study incorporated efficacy endpoints, including response rate per International Myeloma Working Group (IMWG) response criteria, MRD negativity, PFS and duration of response. Due to a lack of progression events at the time of data cutoff, additional planned secondary endpoints not reported in this paper are PFS after initiation of active myeloma therapy and time to progression. Efficacy was assessed in the overall cohort and in prespecified dose strata, with 12 patients receiving 0.3−0.5 × 106 and eight patients receiving ≥0.5 × 106 CAR+ T cells per kilogram.

At a median follow-up of 15.3 months, all patients achieved MRD negativity at a sensitivity threshold of 10−6 by next-generation sequencing (NGS) (Fig. 2a,b). The median time to MRD negativity was 1 month. All patients had MRD negativity at 10−5 at month 1 and at 10−6 at month 2 after infusion (Fig. 2b). MRD negativity at 10−6 was sustained in subsequent assessments (months 3, 6, 12 and 24) through the data cutoff.

All patients achieved an objective response, and 18 (90%) had a complete or stringent complete response (sCR/CR) as best response (Fig. 2a). In the remaining two patients with approximately 3.5 months of follow-up, immunofixation remains positive, most likely due to slow clearance of residual paraprotein. Among the 16 patients with at least 6 months of follow-up, all 16 (100%) achieved sCR/CR.

No disease progression or deaths were observed; thus, median progression-free and overall survival were not reached (Fig. 2c,d). No difference in response or response durability was observed in high-risk versus standard-risk cytogenetics.

Secondary outcomes: biomarkers of antimyeloma and immune activity

As part of the secondary outcomes, we analyzed three biomarkers of cilta-cel treatment: post-infusion lymphocyte expansion measured by ALC, soluble BCMA (sBCMA) and longitudinal CAR T cell immunophenotype, including CD4:CD8 ratios and CAR T cell subsets (Supplementary Table 3).

We compared laboratory kinetics during the first 21 days after cilta-cel infusion by dose group and NINT status. During this period, 18 of 20 patients reached ALC > 3.0 × 109 per liter. The median peak ALC across the cohort was 6.8 × 109 per liter (range, 2.4–36.4 × 109) and was numerically higher in patients with >0.5 × 106 CAR+ T cells per kilogram (12.4 × 109 versus 6.4 × 109 per liter; P = 0.17) and in those with NINTs (12.7 × 109 versus 6.3 × 109 per liter; P = 0.08). ALC exposure and kinetics did not differ significantly across dose groups (area under the concentration–time curve (AUC), P = 0.12; trajectory, P = 0.10; Fig. 3a). By contrast, patients who developed NINTs showed greater post-infusion lymphocyte expansion, with higher ALC exposure (AUC, P = 0.006; Fig. 3b) and a distinct longitudinal ALC pattern compared to those without NINTs (trajectory, P < 0.0001), including after adjustment for dose (Supplementary Table 4a and Extended Data Fig. 3a). Ferritin and interleukin-6 trajectories were associated with NINT status but not dose group, whereas C-reactive protein trajectories differed by both NINT status and dose group (Extended Data Fig. 3b−d and Supplementary Table 4b−d).

Additionally, we assessed sBCMA as an orthogonal, serum-based marker of myeloma burden. After CAR T cell infusion, sBCMA declined rapidly, independent of dose, and fell below the median value in healthy controls by day 21 (Fig. 3c). By day 56, 18 of 20 patients were below the control median, and, by day 100, 19 of 20 patients were below the control median (Extended Data Fig. 4). Baseline sBCMA did not correlate with increased incidence of NINTs.

Longitudinal flow cytometry profiling through day 100 showed that patients receiving >0.5 × 106 CAR T cells per kilogram achieved higher peak absolute CAR+ T cell counts than those treated at the reduced dose (median 9,564 (IQR, 3,691–17,665) versus 2,054 (IQR, 844–4,102) cells per microliter) (Fig. 3d and Extended Data Fig. 5a). In addition, CAR T cell expansion kinetics differed between patients with versus without NINTs (Fig. 3e and Supplementary Table 4e). CAR+ cells remained detectable in 10 of 20 patients at day 56 and in nine of 20 patients at day 100 (Extended Data Fig. 5b).

The CD4:CD8 T cell ratio has been associated with clinical outcomes in RRMM25,26. In the present study, the ratio did not differ over days 5–21 when summarized by AUC by dose group but was higher in patients who developed NINTs compared to those who did not (Fig. 3f,g). At peak expansion, phenotypic composition was broadly similar across dose groups, with T effector memory (TEM) cells predominating among total CAR+ cells and within the CAR+ CD4 and CD8 subsets (Extended Data Fig. 6). To assess longitudinal phenotypic trajectories, we summarized T cell phenotypes across patients with detectable CAR+ T cells through day 100 and found that the proportion of TEM cells decreased over time to approximately 56–60% in the low-dose and NINT-negative groups, accompanied by an increase in terminally differentiated T effector memory cells reexpressing CD45RA (TEMRA) and T central memory (TCM) cells, whereas, in NINT-positive patients, the TEM cell fraction remained high (≥81.5%) with correspondingly lower contributions from TEMRA and TCM cells (Fig. 3h,i).

Discussion

In this phase 2 trial of cilta-cel in HR-SMM, a single cilta-cel infusion administered without induction or bridging therapy produced rapid, deep responses, with MRD negativity at 10−6 achieved in all patients and sustained through the data cutoff. The uniformity and sustainability of MRD-negative responses is notable and raises the possibility that finite cellular therapy may achieve durable disease control when administered at an earlier, asymptomatic stage. A longer follow-up will be required to determine durability and to assess whether these responses translate into long-term freedom from progression.

The safety profile was consistent with lymphodepleting chemotherapy and cilta-cel therapy, with no new safety signals. CRS occurred in all patients and was limited to grade 1 or 2. No IEC-HS, IEC-enterocolitis or secondary malignancies were observed to date. Prolonged cytopenias and severe infection-related complications, important sources of morbidity in RRMM CAR T cell cohorts27, were not prominent in this precursor setting. This likely reflects limited prior treatment exposure, including the absence of prior high-dose melphalan, with preservation of hematopoietic reserve.

Biomarker analyses suggested that NINTs were associated with greater post-infusion expansion of lymphocytes and CAR T cells and a higher CD4:CD8 ratio. Although elevated post-infusion ALC may represent an early clinical correlate of subsequent NINT risk in this setting, these findings are exploratory and should be interpreted with caution in this smoldering population.

Patients with >40% bone marrow plasmacytosis were excluded, and induction or bridging therapy was not permitted to preserve a true ‘one-and-done’ strategy of CAR T cell therapy, allowing direct assessment of its independent therapeutic effect. The lack of an association between baseline sBCMA levels and NINTs suggests that disease burden alone may not fully capture the risk of neurotoxicity. However, this finding should be interpreted cautiously and does not negate a potential role for pre-infusion cytoreduction. Collectively, the data point toward a multifactorial etiology of NINTs involving both disease-related and host-related determinants, such as T cell quality.

We note that a proactive approach was used for CRS and NINTs. Although most events were grade 1, interventions were initiated to prevent escalation and promote early control of symptoms. Accordingly, the use of these interventions should be interpreted as reflecting a deliberate preemptive management strategy rather than the presence of higher-grade toxicity.

Notably, CAR+ T cells displayed an effector memory-skewed phenotype, which differs from reports in the relapsed/refractory setting. Although this profile was observed through day 100 in our dataset, the limited number of late timepoint samples with sufficiently detectable CAR+ T cells precludes firm conclusions.

The depth of response compares favorably with prior early-intervention approaches in HR-SMM. In the GEM-CESAR trial of carfilzomib, lenalidomide and dexamethasone followed by autologous transplantation and lenalidomide maintenance, MRD negativity was achieved in approximately 60% of patients, with sustained MRD negativity at 4 years in 31%28. MRD-negative rates with other triplet and quadruplet regimens have generally been lower than those observed here in both SMM and newly diagnosed multiple myeloma17,18,29,30,31. The efficacy of cilta-cel in this HR-SMM cohort also appears greater than that typically observed in RRMM cohorts, including patients treated earlier in relapse (for example, CARTITUDE-4)1,3, supporting the hypothesis that immune fitness and disease biology at earlier stages may improve outcomes.

This trial provides initial evidence that a reduced cilta-cel dose (0.3 × 106 CAR+ T cells per kilogram) is sufficient to induce uniformly deep, MRD-negative responses in HR-SMM, with response kinetics similar to those observed at higher dose levels. Although post-infusion lymphocyte expansion appeared attenuated in the reduced-dose cohort, expansion nevertheless occurred despite preemptive dexamethasone prophylaxis, which we interpret as reflecting a more intact and immunologically competent host environment in earlier disease states.

Limitations of this study include the single-arm design, single-center experience and small sample size. Follow-up is still limited for a precursor plasma cell disorder, and longer observation will be required to assess the durability of MRD-negative remissions and the effect of treatment on progression to symptomatic myeloma. Moreover, the results from this study cannot be extrapolated to the newly diagnosed or relapsed multiple myeloma populations, given important differences in disease burden, immune fitness and prior therapy.

In this first prospective evaluation of CAR T cell therapy in a precursor plasma cell disorder, treatment with cilta-cel produced uniformly deep and sustained MRD-negative remissions in patients with HR-SMM. Taken together, these findings highlight the potential of early BCMA-directed cellular therapy in this setting and support continued evaluation to better define strategies that optimize benefit–risk balance. An extended follow-up will be essential to determine whether early cellular therapy can modify long-term clinical outcomes in HR-SMM.

Methods

Trial design and patient selection

CAR-PRISM is a single-center phase 2 study evaluating the efficacy and safety of cilta-cel in patients with HR-SMM. Planned dosing included an initial safety run-in (six patients; standard 3 + 3 design) to evaluate escalating dose levels (0.5 × 106 and 0.5–1.0 × 106 viable CAR+ T cells per kilogram), followed by dose expansion at the protocol-specified target dose (0.5–1.0 × 106 viable CAR+ T cells per kilogram) (Fig. 1b). During the safety run-in, enrollment was staggered at 6-week intervals, with a protocol-defined safety pause after the first three patients to allow safety assessment before further enrollment and dose escalation. After completion of the safety run-in, enrollment was paused for FDA review before the expansion phase was initiated.

Due to emerging data that peak ALC may be associated with increased risk of neurological toxicities, along with low threshold for NINTs in this population, the protocol was amended and the final seven patients received 0.3 × 106 viable CAR+ T cells per kilogram, with enrollment of one patient every 30 days.

Eligible participants were adults with high-risk SMM and bone marrow plasma cell involvement of 40% or less, identified during a screening period of up to 28 days. This cutoff was prospectively selected as a pragmatic safety measure for this first study of cilta-cel in a precursor plasma cell disorder, to limit baseline disease burden and potentially reduce the risk of excessive CAR T cell expansion and treatment-related toxicity in the absence of induction or bridging therapy. HR-SMM was defined according to established clinical risk models, including the 20−2−20 criteria or an equivalent validated scoring system, and could also be established in patients with at least 10% bone marrow plasma cells plus additional high-risk features such as evolving disease, adverse plasma cell immunophenotype with immunoparesis, high-risk cytogenetic abnormalities (t(4;14), t(14;16), t(14;20), del(17p), TP53 mutation, 1q21 gain or del(13q)) or marked urinary light chain excretion7,8,10,11,12. Patients were required to have no evidence of symptomatic multiple myeloma or myeloma-defining events; an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status of 0 or 1; and adequate hematologic, hepatic and renal function; they were also required to provide written informed consent. Participants of reproductive potential were required to have a negative pregnancy test, use effective contraception and refrain from gamete donation for 1 year after cilta-cel infusion. Participants also had to be willing to comply with protocol-specific monitoring requirements, including remaining within close proximity to the treating center after CAR T cell infusion.

Key exclusion criteria included prior SMM-directed therapy within 6 months, any history of symptomatic multiple myeloma or prior therapy for active myeloma, prior anti-BCMA therapy, recent anticancer therapy or vaccination within protocol-defined washout periods, active central nervous system involvement, myelodysplastic syndrome, uncontrolled infection or other uncontrolled intercurrent illness, significant cardiac disease, recent stroke or seizure, recent major surgery, pregnancy or breastfeeding and serious medical, neurologic or psychiatric conditions that could interfere with study participation or safety. Patients with another active malignancy were excluded unless the malignancy was considered cured or at very low risk of recurrence. Active viral infection with HIV, hepatitis B virus, hepatitis C virus or SARS-CoV-2 was generally exclusionary, although patients with controlled or resolved infection meeting protocol-specified virologic criteria could be enrolled.

After leukapheresis, cilta-cel was manufactured by Johnson & Johnson using a centralized process; the administered dose was adjusted at Dana-Farber Cancer Institute. All patients received lymphodepleting chemotherapy with fludarabine (30 mg m−2 per day) and cyclophosphamide (300 mg m−2 per day) administered for three consecutive days, followed by 2 days of rest before cilta-cel infusion.

The first 15 patients were hospitalized for post-infusion monitoring, with longitudinal follow-up for safety, efficacy and adverse events. The remaining cohort of patients received cilta-cel infusion in the outpatient setting and were hospitalized only at the onset of adverse events (for example, CRS).

Participant characteristics and sex/gender reporting

A total of 20 treated participants were included in this study. Participant age and sex are summarized in Table 1. Sex was recorded from the clinical record as part of standard study enrollment procedures. Gender identity was not collected. Sex and gender were considered in study reporting in accordance with journal guidance. Given the small sample size and the limited number of outcome events, the study was not designed or powered to detect sex-based or gender-based differences in efficacy, toxicity or correlative biomarkers; therefore, no formal sex-statified or gender-stratified analyses were performed. Participants were not financially compensated for participation in this investigator-initiated clinical study beyond protocol-specified clinical care and study procedures.

Ethics approval and consent

The trial was conducted in accordance with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki. The protocol was approved by the institutional review board of the Dana-Farber Cancer Institute (protocol 22-546). All patients provided written informed consent before enrollment. The trial was registered at ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT05767359. A redacted study protocol is available as a supplementary file.

Study oversight

The study was designed by the authors in collaboration with the sponsor. The authors collected the data, performed the analyses and interpreted the results. The authors vouch for the accuracy and completeness of the data and for the fidelity of the study to the protocol.

Primary endpoint

The primary endpoint was safety, assessed by the incidence of DLTs during the first 60 days after cilta-cel infusion. DLTs were defined according to prespecified protocol criteria and included severe non-hematologic toxic effects, CRS, neurologic toxic effects or prolonged cytopenias (Study Protocol).

Neurologic toxic effects were classified as ICANS or NINTs, defined as neurologic adverse events not meeting consensus ICANS criteria. Adverse events were graded according to CTCAE version 5, and CRS and ICANS were graded according to established consensus criteria32.

Secondary endpoints

Secondary endpoints included the proportion of patients achieving a complete response or better after treatment initiation; MRD negativity at 6 months, 1 year and 2 years; PFS until progression to multiple myeloma; PFS after initiation of active myeloma therapy (PFS2); time to progression; and duration of response. Responses were assessed according to IMWG criteria. MRD was evaluated in bone marrow aspirates using an NGS-based assay (Adaptive Biotechnologies). Additional secondary endpoints included successful manufacturing, pharmacokinetic measures of CAR T cell expansion and persistence, pharmacodynamic biomarkers of cilta-cel antimyeloma and immune activity and immunogenicity of cilta-cel in SMM.

Assays for correlative studies

Multiparametric flow cytometry

Immunophenotypic analyses of fresh or cryopreserved peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) from patients and timepoints were performed by flow cytometry. We used two different validated fluorochrome-conjugated surface monoclonal antibody panels, as detailed in Supplementary Table 5. Antibodies were used in accordance with manufacturer validation and established flow cytometry practice. Staining conditions were optimized using standard assay controls.

Cryopreserved PBMCs were rapidly thawed and washed with RPMI containing 10% FBS, and, later, PBS containing 2% FBS was used as the main buffer for the next steps. Fresh blood samples were incubated with lysis buffer for 15 minutes and washed with lysis buffer to remove red blood cells. Once the cells were washed and resuspended, BCMA CAR Detection Reagent was added for 15 minutes, followed by addition of directly conjugated monoclonal antibodies, and incubated for an additional 15 minutes at 4 °C. After this incubation, cells were washed twice and stained with a viability marker before analysis by flow cytometry. An LSRFortessa flow cytometer (BD Biosciences) was used for data acquisition. FACSDiva and FlowJo (version 10) software (BD Biosciences) were used for data analysis. Gating strategies and antibodies relevant for this study are shown in Supplementary Fig. 1 and Supplementary Table 5.

sBCMA measurements

Plasma samples from patients in the CAR-PRISM trial were stored at −30 °C until analysis. Frozen samples were thawed and sequentially diluted at least three times (range, 1:5−1:400). sBCMA levels were then measured using an ELISA (Human BCMA/TNFRSF17 DuoSet ELISA DY193; R&D Systems) according to the manufacturer’s protocol and as previously published33,34,35,36,37,38. The ELISA plates were read by a SpectraMax M3 Multi-Mode Microplate Reader (Molecular Devices Inc.) at 450 nm using SoftMax Pro data acquisition and analysis software (version 7.1). sBCMA levels are reported as the mean of duplicate samples measured across three sequential dilutions for each specimen, expressed in ng ml−1; 20 healthy donor cryopreserved plasma samples (age range, 19−63 years; median, 49 years) were analyzed as a control validation cohort.

Statistical analysis

Sample size justification

Because the primary objective was safety, no formal hypothesis-based sample size justification was performed for the safety endpoint. The planned sample size of 20 participants was instead determined by a prespecified single-stage exact binomial design for efficacy, in which a complete response rate of 40% was considered promising and 5% was considered non-promising; the regimen was considered successful if at least four complete responses were observed and non-promising if three or fewer complete responses were observed. This design provided >89% power with a one-sided type I error of 0.016, and complete response was summarized as a proportion with exact binomial 90% and 99% confidence intervals.

Analysis of patient data and outcomes

All treated participants were included in the safety and efficacy analyses. Safety outcomes, including adverse events and neurotoxicity events, were summarized descriptively. Response outcomes were summarized as proportions with exact binomial confidence intervals. Continuous variables were summarized as medians with ranges and categorical variables as counts and percentages. Associations between categorical and continuous variables were assessed using the Wilcoxon rank-sum test or the Kruskal–Wallis test, as appropriate, and associations between categorical variables were assessed using Fisher’s exact test. Time-to-event outcomes were estimated using the Kaplan–Meier method, with confidence intervals calculated using Greenwood’s formula; patients without an event were censored at the date of last follow-up. Median follow-up was estimated using the reverse Kaplan–Meier method. All statistical tests were two-sided unless otherwise specified, and P< 0.05 was considered nominally significant. No formal adjustment for multiple testing was applied except where explicitly stated for exploratory correlative analyses. Missing data were not imputed. Statistical analyses were performed in R software (version 4.4.1), using the packages survival (version 3.7-0), survminer (version 0.4.9), dplyr (version 1.1.4), tidyr (version 1.3.1), ggplot2 (version 4.0.2), prodlim (version 2024.06.25) and Hmisc (version 5.2-4).

AUC analyses for correlative studies

For each correlative biomarker, we summarized early post-CAR T trajectories using the AUC. AUC was calculated per patient using the trapezoidal rule from observed biomarker values plotted against time (days from infusion). The intended analysis window was days 0–21; however, when sampling density within days 0–21 was insufficient to support a stable estimate, the window was extended to days 0–28 to incorporate additional available measurements. AUC calculations were restricted to measurements within the selected window, and patients contributed an AUC only if they had adequate data within that window (prespecified minimum number of post-infusion measurements). Biomarker distributions were assessed, and, where appropriate, values were log transformed to reduce skewness prior to AUC computation and/or statistical testing. Group comparisons of AUC (for example, by neurotoxicity status and/or dose group) were performed using two-sided non-parametric tests (Wilcoxon rank-sum) unless model assumptions supported parametric testing. For analyses spanning multiple biomarkers, results were interpreted in an exploratory framework, and multiplicity adjustment was applied when specified (for example, Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate). AUC results are reported as median (IQR) by group. Analyses were performed in R (version 4.4.1) using dplyr (version 1.1.4) and tidyr (version 1.3.1).

Longitudinal mixed-effects modeling

To compare longitudinal trajectories of laboratory and correlative biomarkers over time by CAR T cell dose group and neurotoxicity status, we fitted linear mixed-effects models with patient-specific random intercepts to account for repeated measurements within individuals. Outcomes were log transformed where appropriate to improve distributional assumptions and stabilize variance. Time (days from infusion) was modeled flexibly using a natural cubic spline with 4 degrees of freedom. Fixed effects included the spline term for time; main effects for NINT status (yes versus no) and dose group (>0.5 × 106 versus 0.3–0.5 × 106 CAR+ viable T cells per kilogram); and interactions of time with NINT status and dose group to allow group-specific temporal trajectories. Models were fit using restricted maximum likelihood. Statistical evidence for each fixed-effect term was assessed using type III F-tests with Satterthwaite’s approximation for denominator degrees of freedom as implemented in the lmerTest package. Two-sided P values are reported. Models were fitted in R (version 4.4.1) using lme4 (version 1.1-35.5) and lmerTest (version 3.1-3), with spline terms generated using splines (version 4.4.1).
