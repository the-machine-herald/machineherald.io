UK land use implications of replacing dairy farming with precision fermentation

Abstract

Reducing animal agriculture is key to releasing land needed to achieve global environmental goals. Precision fermentation (PF) is a technology which uses genetically engineered microbes to produce ingredients such as dairy proteins. Few studies have quantified PF’s land-sparing potential. Using the UK as a case study, we assess the land use implications of scaling up the production of PF milk (plant-based milk containing PF dairy protein), by calculating and summing land footprints (area/mass) of PF milk’s individual components. PF milk’s land footprint was estimated to be as much as 96% lower than that of cow’s milk. Replacing 20%, 50% or 100% of UK-produced cow’s milk with PF milk was estimated to release 859 kha, 2,147 kha or 4,294 kha of land by 2050. This corresponds to 40–202% of required permanent grassland and 16–82% of required cropland to meet the UK Climate Change Committee’s 2050 ‘Balanced Net Zero’ targets regarding afforestation, bioenergy, peatland restoration (lowland grassland only), agroforestry and hedgerow creation. These results suggest PF could play a critical role in releasing land for net zero measures and alleviating land use pressures in the UK and globally. Further research is required to address knowledge gaps and facilitate more nuanced assessments of PF’s land-sparing potential.

1 Introduction

There are growing global pressures on land to meet demands for food, climate mitigation and settlement growth (Chatham House, 2023). Just under half (44%) of global habitable land is currently used for agriculture (Ritchie and Roser, 2024), of which 83% is associated with the production of animal products, through grazing livestock and growing their feed (Poore and Nemecek, 2018). However, animal products constitute only 18% of global calories and 38% of protein (ibid). Raising livestock and producing their feed also accounts for nearly 60% of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from global food production, and is a leading driver of biodiversity loss (Xu et al., 2021; Dimbleby, 2021).

Alternative proteins (APs) are gaining attention as a means of achieving a reduction in animal agriculture, and thus its associated land use demands and environmental impacts. Precision fermentation (PF) is emerging as a particularly promising AP technology. It involves genetically engineering microbes (commonly yeast or bacteria) to contain the genes encoding specific functional ingredients, including animal proteins (GFI, 2022). The microbes are then cultivated in bioreactors with a carbohydrate source, typically a sugar-based feedstock (e.g., sucrose or glucose), which they convert into the desired ingredient (Grossmann, 2024; Fletcher et al., 2024). For a more detailed description of this process see Augustin et al. (2024).

While plant-based substitutes – products produced directly from plants (GFI, 2022) – can differ in sensory properties or nutritional profile from conventional animal products, products of PF are bioidentical to their animal-derived counterparts (Knychala et al., 2024). The technology is well-established, having been used in the food industry since the 1990s to produce chymosin, an enzyme in rennet which used to be exclusively derived from calves’ stomach linings (ibid). PF has more recently been used to produce APs, including milk and egg proteins (Nielsen et al., 2024). The process of producing beta-lactoglobulin (B-LG) via PF is more developed than that of other APs, with several businesses including US-based Perfect Day producing its animal-free B-LG at commercial scales (Fletcher et al., 2024). B-LG is the major whey protein in cow’s milk, constituting 10–15% of total milk protein (Aich et al., 2015). 80% of the protein in cow’s milk is casein, and 20% is whey (Davoodi et al., 2016).

By avoiding raising animals and growing their feed, the land-sparing potential of PF APs could be substantial. However, existing assessments of PF’s environmental performance have primarily focused on GHG emissions (Nielsen et al., 2024; Behm et al., 2022; Sinistore et al., 2021). Only one life cycle assessment (LCA) published in a peer-reviewed journal was identified which assessed the land use of PF APs: an anticipatory LCA of PF ovalbumin (the major egg white protein) produced at industrial scales in Finland, Germany and Poland (Järviö et al., 2021). The land use of PF ovalbumin was found to be ~89% lower than that of chicken egg-derived ovalbumin (4.1–4.5m2/kg relative to 36–45 m2/kg) (ibid). Several PF businesses have published independent LCAs of their products, although only one was identified which assessed the land area required to produce a given amount of product. This was an LCA of US business Tomorrow Farms’ ‘Bored Cow’ animal-free milk, which derives its protein from PF B-LG (Planet FWD, 2023). The LCA claims Bored Cow milk’s land use to be 0.016m2/kg, 96% lower than that of milk produced by cows (0.374 m2/kg). A Green Alliance report by Collas and Benton (2024) estimated the land area that could be released in 10 European countries by scaling up APs including PF products, as well as plant-based alternatives and cultivated meat [genuine animal meat produced using animal cells grown in vitro (GFI, 2022)]. The 10 countries were the UK, France, Spain, Germany, Italy, Denmark, Sweden, Poland, Romania and the Netherlands. The authors found that, if APs replaced two-thirds of meat and dairy consumption in these countries by 2050, 44% of their current agricultural land would be released. Overseas land used to produce imported food would also reduce by 57%, freeing up an area the size of Spain (Collas and Benton, 2024). The land footprint (hectares (ha)/metric ton (t)) of PF dairy protein used in this report was taken from Sillman et al. (2019), a study non-specific to dairy proteins, which assessed a form of biomass fermentation rather than PF. In biomass fermentation, the desired end-product is the entire microbial biomass rather than one specific protein as in PF. The production process assessed in Sillman et al. (2019) also used a gaseous feedstock, and hence did not require agricultural land to grow feedstock crops. Instead, this land footprint encompassed the land needed for production facilities, and for the renewable energy and related infrastructure used to power the process.

The lack of understanding around the land use implications of PF APs is an important international knowledge gap that requires addressing. This is particularly true for dairy, given that commercial PF efforts are primarily centered around whey (Fletcher et al., 2024). The UK is the tenth largest milk producer globally (Ritchie et al., 2023), producing approximately 15 billion litres of milk annually from a herd of 1.84 million cows (Defra, 2024a). This represents around 1.6% of annual global milk production (Ritchie et al., 2023). Dairy is the UK’s largest agricultural sector, with milk production valued at approximately £6 billion. Around 42% of milk produced in the UK is consumed as liquid milk. The remainder is processed into dairy products, the majority of which (61%) becomes cheese (Defra, 2024a).

According to government statistics, approximately 69% of UK land is used for agriculture, of which 85% is associated with livestock production (Defra, 2025b). The land used for livestock is further divided into approximately 75% used for grazing and 25% for feed crops (Defra, 2025a). However, no breakdown of land use by dairy or other livestock sectors is provided. In the UK, the most common dairy cow management system uses a combination of grazing and indoor housing (March et al., 2014). Approximately 30% of dairy farms maintain a system of 100% outdoor grazing during summer, while an estimated 5–20% house cattle indoors permanently (WWF, 2022).

The UK Government has identified APs as an important area for research and development in support of the country’s future food system. The UK Climate Change Committee (CCC), a statutory body advising the UK Government on climate change policy, has called for reductions of between 20% and 50% in conventional dairy production by 2050, with replacements by plant-based or novel protein alternatives, including PF (CCC, 2020, 2025). The CCC identifies these reductions as necessary to both reduce the GHG emissions from the dairy sector and to release land for onward land use measures. These measures are needed to sequester carbon (i.e., via woodland creation (afforestation), hedgerow creation, and integrating trees on arable land and grassland (agroforestry)), to reduce carbon emissions (i.e., via peatland restoration), or to replace fossil fuels (by growing bioenergy crops for use with carbon capture and storage – i.e., short rotation coppice (SRC), short rotation forestry (SRF) and Miscanthus).

The aim of this study was to assess the potential land use implications of producing PF dairy at scale to replace milk from dairy cattle (also referred to here as “conventional milk”), using the UK as a case study. We did this by modelling different levels of ambition, including the dairy reduction scenarios proposed by the CCC to meet net zero by 2050. The resulting land use change was then assessed against the CCC’s targets for land use measures.

2 Materials and methods

2.1 PF milk land footprint

To estimate the annual land area (ha) needed to produce 1 t of PF milk (i.e., the PF milk land footprint), some overarching assumptions were made. Firstly, it was assumed that all PF dairy products (e.g., cheese, yogurt, etc.) can and would be made from PF milk; liquid milk is the largest use of raw cow’s milk produced in the UK, and the primary ingredient from which all dairy products are made (Defra, 2024b). This assumption enabled land footprints of conventional and PF dairy to be compared.

As with conventional milk, PF milk is composed of protein, fat and carbohydrates (sugar), as well as water, vitamins and minerals. It was assumed that the water, vitamins and minerals in PF milk have no land footprint, as per Collas and Benton (2024). Only the milk protein component of PF milk is produced by PF. The PF milk land footprints calculated in our study only concern land used to grow crops to produce PF milk. This comprises the crops used as feedstocks to produce PF milk protein, and the crops used to supply the fat and carbohydrate in PF milk. This assumption ensured consistency and comparability with the cow’s milk land footprint calculated in Supplementary Material Section 2, which only accounted for the agricultural land used to feed dairy cows. Thus, the PF milk footprints do not account for PF production facilities, and neither PF nor cow’s milk land footprints account for processing or distribution facilities. Imported crops are not considered in our calculations. We have also not considered the use of a non-crop-based feedstock for the PF process.

For each crop involved in producing each component of PF milk, land footprints were calculated by considering the highest and lowest crop yields and the proportion of the crop which is utilized to produce PF milk (see Tables 1, 2). The PF land footprints are based on the mass of the crop components used in PF as a proportion of the total mass of all useful crop parts.

This assumption is supported by SB being a feedstock used to produce multiple PF APs, not just B-LG (Grossmann, 2024; Linder, 2023), and that existing studies have also assumed all PF dairy proteins have the same land footprint (Collas and Benton, 2024; Bhandari et al., 2021). As discussed in the Introduction, the production of other dairy proteins via PF is less developed than that of B-LG, and no LCAs of these other proteins were identified.

2.1.5 Sensitivity to rapeseed oil extraction efficiency

In addition to the potential variation in crop yields, and the choice of feedstock crop (SB or maize), we tested the sensitivity of our results to the extraction efficiency of rapeseed oil from OSR. We found that the fat component in the form of rapeseed oil contributed ~79–80% of the PF milk land footprint (see Section 3.1), and as such its extraction efficiency has the greatest impact on the final footprint.

We reduced EF from 0.98 (typical of modern solvent-extraction processes) to 0.85, with the latter reflecting the efficiency of mechanical extraction in the UK in 1999 (Hunt, 1999). This choice provides a deliberately conservative estimate; mechanical extraction efficiencies have likely improved since 1999, and over 70% of global rapeseed oil production uses solvent-extraction methods, which are more efficient (ABC Machinery, 2025).

2.2 Land area released

2.2.1 Dairy farming land area

To estimate the land released by replacing conventional milk with PF milk, the UK grassland area used for grazing dairy cows and the cropland area used for growing their feed was required. This information was calculated using the approach and/or data in de Ruiter et al. (2017). To calculate the grassland area used for grazing dairy cows, the UK’s total permanent grassland area (kha) was allocated across all types of grazing livestock (i.e., sheep, beef and dairy cattle), by multiplying the feed conversion rates/ratios (FCRs) and total annual UK production volumes for each livestock type. FCR is a measure of feed use efficiency, i.e., the mass of feed (kg) needed to produce 1 kg of animal product (Wilkinson, 2011). The sum of the resulting values was used to determine the proportion of permanent grassland each livestock category occupies, which was converted into an area (kha) using the total UK permanent grassland area.

To calculate the cropland area used for growing dairy feed crops, the total UK agricultural land area (i.e., permanent grassland area plus croppable area) was multiplied by the proportion of UK agricultural land which is used to grow livestock feed crops. The resulting value was then multiplied by the proportion of cropland used to grow livestock feed crops that is specifically used to grow dairy feed crops.

The permanent grassland area and cropland area associated with UK dairy farming were then summed to give the total land area used to produce cow’s milk in the UK, which was converted to a land footprint using the volume of raw milk leaving farm (million liters) in 2023.

Supplementary Material Section 2 provides further details of the method taken to calculate the land footprint of UK cow’s milk.

2.3 Climate Change Committee’s land use recommendations

To assess how land released by PF dairy could contribute to the CCC’s net zero land use recommendations in their Sixth Carbon Budget, it was necessary to determine the areas of permanent grassland and cropland that would need to be released by 2050 for the CCC’s net zero land use change (LUC) measures (i.e., afforestation, bioenergy, peatland restoration (lowland grassland only), agroforestry and hedgerow creation), under the BNZ and WE pathways. This information was obtained or calculated from a UK Centre for Ecology & Hydrology (UKCEH) report quantifying the impact of the CCC’s land use scenarios (Thomson et al., 2020) (see Supplementary Material Section 3 for further details). For both BNZ and WE pathways, the permanent grassland/cropland area used for each LUC measure in 2020 was subtracted from the respective 2050 area, to calculate the permanent grassland/cropland area that would need to be released by 2050. The total permanent grassland and total cropland areas required for the CCC’s LUC measures by 2050 were compared with permanent grassland and cropland areas released by PF dairy.

Thomson et al. (2020) distinguish between cropland and temporary grassland, and between permanent grassland and rough grazing (see Supplementary Material Section 3). However, our study assumed that cropland includes temporary grassland, and that permanent grassland includes rough grazing, as per UK government reporting (Defra, 2024a). Our study also assumed the proportions of permanent grassland and cropland required for the hedgerow creation targets are the same as those required for the agroforestry targets, as Thomson et al. (2020) do not provide these proportions for the hedgerow creation targets. Another assumption we made was that any land released by reducing dairy farming would not contribute towards the CCC’s ‘upland grassland’ and ‘lowland cropland’ peatland restoration targets. Lowland peat soils are highly fertile (Lloyd et al., 2023), so are likely to be used to grow food for humans rather than livestock. It was assumed no dairy farming occurs on upland grassland, as this is mostly used for grazing sheep (CCC, 2020). These targets were thus excluded from the analysis. Furthermore, by subtracting the 2020 areas per LUC measure from the respective 2050 areas, it was assumed areas used per LUC measure are the same today as in 2020. This assumption may have led to a slight overestimation of additional land needed by 2050. However, any difference is unlikely to be substantial; according to the CCC (2023) progress report to Parliament, “delivery of woodland and peatland targets is falling substantially short of both the CCC’s Balanced Pathway and Government-set targets. Planting of perennial energy crops has remained static since 2013” (CCC, 2023, p. 243).

The PF milk allocation land footprints estimated in our study are also higher than the reported land footprint of Tomorrow Farms’ PF milk, by 500–750% (Table 9). Tomorrow Farms’ LCA was based on US production systems, which possibly contributed to this disparity, although it is unclear how these land footprints were calculated. Regional production system differences may also partly explain why the cow’s milk land footprint estimated in our study was 695% higher than in Tomorrow Farms’ LCA (Table 9). Compared to the UK, US dairy farming is associated with reduced pasture access and higher milk yields per cow (Crump et al., 2021; AHDB, 2020). It should be noted that the global cow’s milk land footprint estimated by Poore and Nemecek (2018) is considerably closer to that estimated in our study than in Tomorrow Farms’ LCA (Table 9). The percentage decrease between the cow’s milk and PF milk land footprint in Tomorrow Farms’ LCA aligned with that estimated in our study (Table 9).

The estimate of the cow’s milk land footprint reported by de Ruiter et al. (2017) is 33% higher than that estimated in our study (Table 9). Some discrepancy is expected, given that our calculations used more recent UK data on production volumes and national grassland and cropland areas. Furthermore, de Ruiter et al. (2017) estimated the land footprint associated with milk consumed in the UK, rather than milk produced, as in our study.

Our study’s estimate of the cow’s milk land footprint is 143% higher than that reported by Audsley et al. (2009), an LCA of major food commodities consumed in the UK (Table 9). This may be partly due to the approach we used which assigned all UK grasslands to livestock production, including those not necessarily used for this purpose, whereas Audsley et al. (2009) derived their estimates using a bottom-up LCA approach which may not account for the total agricultural land used (e.g., field margins). Again, some of this discrepancy may also be attributable to the fact that Audsley et al. (2009) assessed UK consumption rather than production.

Finally, the Lesschen et al. (2011) estimated cow’s milk land footprint for the EU (including the UK at the time) is only 18% lower than ours and therefore comparable (Table 9). The similarity between findings may be due to both studies assessing production.

No existing literature identified could be directly compared with our study’s estimates of UK land released by PF dairy nor its contribution to the CCC’s LUC measures. However, our results corroborate previous findings that scaling up PF dairy can help achieve substantial reductions in agricultural land use (Collas and Benton, 2024; Mazac et al., 2022; Tubb and Seba, 2019).

4.2 Wider implications

Although this study focused on PF dairy, PF startups are targeting various ingredients, including meat and egg proteins, and fats (GFI, 2023). Hence, PF could potentially help release more UK land by 2050 than was estimated in this study. This section discusses some of the wider implications of releasing substantial land through scaling up PF, including those beyond the UK.

4.2.1 Land use demands

The results of this study suggest PF dairy could play a crucial role in releasing land for UK net zero LUC measures; even the lowest level of replacement of cow’s milk with PF milk (20%) was found to release over a third of UK land required for the CCC’s 2050 LUC measures (BNZ pathway). This highlights how critical it is that future PF dairy LCAs include land use; most existing assessments have focused on GHG emissions, with some findings suggesting GHG emissions from PF B-LG may be comparable to those from conventional B-LG (Behm et al., 2022). Failing to consider land use alongside emissions overlooks the emissions-reducing potential of the land PF could release.

Although this study allocated freed-up land to the CCC’s LUC measures, as these are most suited to agricultural land (Thomson et al., 2020), this would not necessarily happen in practice. Furthermore, if PF milk replaced 100% of cow’s milk, more land could be released than the CCC’s 2050 LUC measures require. This land could be used for various purposes, including rewilding, providing settlement or producing food for the growing UK population. The UK was 62% self-sufficient in overall food production and 53% self-sufficient in vegetable production in 2023, with the latter being the lowest since records began in 1988 (NFU, 2024).

Our study’s findings have broader policy implications. The UK Government’s 2025 Land Use Consultation highlights the need for major land use change to meet environmental goals, yet does not explicitly address the need to reduce the production or consumption of animal products (Defra, 2025c). Given the political sensitivity around dietary change (Wellesley et al., 2015), PF, which can be used to produce identical proteins with potentially much lower land demands than their animal-derived counterparts, could help policymakers navigate this challenge and overcome inertia around reducing animal agriculture.

4.2.2 Global implications

While this study focused on the UK as a case study, net zero is a global ambition; the goal of reaching net zero emissions in the second half of the century is enshrined in the 195-party Paris Agreement (Schleussner et al., 2022). In line with this, the 195 parties have submitted Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs), which detail their plans to reduce GHG emissions. Over half of NDCs reference afforestation, reforestation and revegetation, and approximately a quarter mention agroforestry and land restoration (UNFCCC, 2023).

However, global dairy consumption is rising, particularly in parts of Asia; between 1961 and 2023, per capita milk consumption increased by 2,456% in China and 1,074% in India (Our World in Data, 2025). To meet this growing demand, global milk production is projected to rise by 1.5% annually over the next decade, outpacing most other major agricultural commodities (Van Eenennaam, 2024). Scaling up conventional dairy production to this extent could intensify land use pressures and undermine global progress towards net zero.

The UK currently produces around 15 million metric tons of cow’s milk per year (Defra, 2024a), accounting for just ~1.6% of global annual production (934 million metric tons) (Ritchie et al., 2023). Based on the global land footprint of cow’s milk calculated by Poore and Nemecek (2018) (Table 9), this equates to global milk production using roughly 184 times more land than UK production alone. The global land sparing capacity of PF could therefore be substantial, with the technology potentially playing a critical role in satisfying growing demand for milk while enabling governments across the world to meet their land use and climate targets. However, assessments of the land area that could be released would need to be done on a country-by-country basis, as the land footprints calculated in this study are UK-specific and cannot be applied globally.

It is also worth noting that, while this study specifically focused on land use, a transition away from dairy farming and towards PF could have significant economic, social, health, and wider environmental implications. Such factors were beyond the scope of this study, although future research addressing them would be valuable.

4.3 Limitations and uncertainties

Many assumptions were made throughout this study, largely due to data scarcity. While Materials and Methods provides detailed explanations of assumptions made per section, this section discusses the implications of the study’s main assumptions.

Firstly, to facilitate comparison with conventional dairy, it was assumed all dairy products can and would be made from PF milk. However, PF milk is not biochemically equivalent to conventional cow’s milk, due to differences in non-protein components, including a notable discrepancy in saturated fat content. This biochemical difference may impact the feasibility of directly substituting PF milk in all traditional dairy applications. Furthermore, not all dairy products use all components of milk. For example, milk has whey removed before being converted into hard cheeses (Robinson and Wilbey, 1998). As more research is conducted into the production of specific PF dairy products, more nuanced assessments of PF dairy land footprints can be made.

The study was also limited by data availability around the sugar-to-protein conversion ratio of PF dairy proteins produced at scale. The only estimates of this ratio identified in the literature were for B-LG (Fletcher et al., 2024), and were based on the PF process described in Behm et al. (2022). Thus, it was assumed these ratios were reliable estimates of those achievable in 2050, not just for B-LG, but for all dairy proteins. As more data becomes available regarding the production of other PF dairy proteins, the PF land footprints calculated in this study may require revision.

Several assumptions were also made regarding the crops that would provide each component of PF milk. While we assumed the carbohydrate and fat components would come from SB and OSR respectively, other crops could be used, which may have different yields and therefore different land footprints. By 2050, the fat in PF milk may not even be derived from plant oils, and may instead be produced via PF or cell culture (Ro, 2023). How this could affect the fat land footprint is unclear.

Similarly, the PF feedstock may be derived from crops other than SB. It is also possible that more environmentally sustainable feedstocks could be used, including side streams from forestry, agriculture or food production (Godfray et al., 2024). Using such feedstocks could further reduce the land footprints calculated in this study. Nevertheless, their economic viability and feasibility has been questioned; antimicrobial compounds in certain side streams render them unsuitable for some microbial hosts unless removed, which can be costly (Grossmann, 2024). Gaseous feedstocks, such as hydrogen and carbon dioxide, may also be used to produce PF dairy proteins at scale (ibid). Such feedstocks have been successfully used to produce various food and feed ingredients, but have thus far received little attention in relation to PF APs. While gaseous feedstocks could drastically reduce PF land footprints, concerns include cost and availability of green hydrogen (ibid). Also, the use of gaseous feedstocks could result in the decoupling of conventional agriculture and PF. This may not be feasible in practice; recent research suggests, at least for cultivated meat, the technology’s success will depend on opportunities for farmers being provided (Royal Agricultural University, 2024).

Our study also assumed the remaining parts of the crops involved in producing PF milk were used rather than wasted. As current uses are well-established, allocation land footprints were deemed more realistic estimates than non-allocation footprints. However, if future production systems cease using some of the crop residues, land footprints could be closer to non-allocation estimates.

Our study assumed the microbial biomass produced during PF is wasted. Studies have highlighted the importance of utilizing this biomass, from both an environmental and economic perspective (Eastham and Leman, 2024; Järviö et al., 2021). Possible uses as food, feed or fertilizer have been proposed (Eastham and Leman, 2024), although the GM nature of the biomass presents valorization challenges (Järviö et al., 2021). Due to uncertainties around potential future uses, these were not factored into our study, which may have led to an overestimation of the 2050 PF milk land footprint.

Several assumptions were made when calculating the cow’s milk land footprint using the approach and/or data from de Ruiter et al. (2017). Notably, it was assumed that the proportion of agricultural land associated with livestock feed, and the proportion of this land which is associated with dairy feed, are the same for both UK consumption (including overseas land) and UK production. Additionally, the approach we used assigned all UK permanent grassland to livestock production based on relative FCRs and annual production volumes, which may have resulted in an overestimation of the cow’s milk land footprint. Nevertheless, we would still expect the cow’s milk land footprint to be substantially higher than that of PF milk.

5 Conclusion

The results of this study estimate the land footprint of PF milk to be 94–96% lower than that of cow’s milk in the UK. Thus, scaling up the production of PF milk could release substantial areas of UK land. Replacing 20%, 50% or 100% of UK cow’s milk with PF milk is expected to release 851–866 kha, 2,128–2,165 kha or 4,257–4,330 kha of land by 2050, respectively. These areas correspond to 40–202% of permanent grassland and ~16–150% of cropland that need to be released by 2050 to meet the UK Climate Change Committee’s land use recommendations regarding afforestation, bioenergy, peatland restoration (lowland grassland only), agroforestry and hedgerow creation.

These findings highlight the key role PF could play in freeing up UK land for net zero measures. It is critical that future PF LCAs include land use (which is absent from several existing PF dairy LCAs), as excluding it risks overlooking this opportunity. Additionally, the results suggest PF dairy could help mitigate future land use pressures, by freeing up land which could be used for various purposes, including rewilding, settlement and/or food production. Although this study focused on PF dairy, PF startups are targeting various ingredients, including meat and egg proteins, and fats (GFI, 2023). Thus, the land PF could help release by 2050 could be even greater than that estimated in this study.

The production of PF dairy proteins is an emerging area of science, with significant knowledge gaps. This study was thus limited by data availability, which required various assumptions throughout. Future research should focus on addressing the following knowledge gaps:

  • Land footprints of all PF dairy products (e.g., cheese, cream etc.)

  • Sugar-to-protein conversion ratios of all dairy proteins that would be used in PF products

  • Feasibility of different PF feedstocks to produce dairy proteins, and sources of non-protein components of PF dairy products

  • Potential uses of the microbial biomass produced during PF

This preliminary assessment of the UK land use implications of PF dairy suggests the technology could play a key role in the transition to a more sustainable food system. As more research emerges addressing the identified knowledge gaps, more nuanced and robust assessments of PF’s land-sparing potential can be made, both for the UK and globally.
