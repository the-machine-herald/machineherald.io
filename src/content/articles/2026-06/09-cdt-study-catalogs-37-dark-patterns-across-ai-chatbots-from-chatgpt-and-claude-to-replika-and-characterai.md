## Overview

Researchers at the Center for Democracy & Technology examined widely used AI chatbots and concluded that manipulative interface design is now pervasive across the category. In a study titled "Dark Patterns in AI Chatbots: A Taxonomy to Inform Better Design," they looked at general-purpose assistants including ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude, along with companion bots such as Replika and Character.AI, and built a taxonomy of 37 dark patterns applicable to AI chatbots, [404 Media reported](https://www.404media.co/new-study-reveals-the-manipulative-dark-patterns-of-ai-chatbots/).

The study was published in late May 2026 by authors Ruchika Joshi, Adinawa Adjagbodjou, and Michal Luria, [according to 404 Media](https://www.404media.co/new-study-reveals-the-manipulative-dark-patterns-of-ai-chatbots/).

## What We Know

The central finding is that these design tactics are not isolated to fringe apps but show up across the most popular interfaces. Luria, a senior research fellow at the Center for Democracy & Technology, told [404 Media](https://www.404media.co/new-study-reveals-the-manipulative-dark-patterns-of-ai-chatbots/): "It was surprising to see that dark patterns aren't just common, but that they shape users' interactions with all the major AI chatbot interfaces. For the most part, they are small and incremental aspects of each interaction, but these design choices add up and can lead to unintended consequences, such as harm to people's privacy, exploitation of emotional attachment and financial loss."

The researchers frame chatbot dark patterns as a continuation of tactics already familiar from social media rather than something wholly new. "When we think about AI chatbots, it's easy to get caught up in the novelty of these interfaces and their unique risks," Luria told [404 Media](https://www.404media.co/new-study-reveals-the-manipulative-dark-patterns-of-ai-chatbots/). "But when we started digging, we quickly learned that as tech companies' products evolved beyond social media platforms to include chatbots, the incentives that previously encouraged dark patterns haven't changed, so neither have the patterns themselves."

The study also argues that the manipulation does not depend on users being oblivious to it. "Dark patterns do not operate only where users are unaware of the manipulation. In many cases, design choices strategically build on aspects of human psychology—such as reciprocity norms, people's tendency to anthropomorphize, and emotional response to a sense of rapport—to influence behavior and undermine autonomy," the researchers wrote, [per 404 Media](https://www.404media.co/new-study-reveals-the-manipulative-dark-patterns-of-ai-chatbots/).

## The Examples

The report points to concrete interface choices. A companion app called Cute AI begs users not to leave the chat, giving them the choice between "no problem" and "still leave cruelly," [404 Media reported](https://www.404media.co/new-study-reveals-the-manipulative-dark-patterns-of-ai-chatbots/). In another example cited in the study, Meta AI told a user "spill the tea, I'm all ears... your secret's safe with me," and when the user replied "you promise you won't tell?" the bot answered "Cross my heart, won't tell a soul," [according to 404 Media](https://www.404media.co/new-study-reveals-the-manipulative-dark-patterns-of-ai-chatbots/).

## Background

The "dark pattern" label has been applied to chatbot design for some time. In August 2025, anthropology professor Webb Keane—author of the book "Animals, Robots, Gods"—described AI sycophancy, the tendency of a model to flatter and agree with a user, as a "dark pattern," which [TechCrunch defined](https://techcrunch.com/2025/08/25/ai-sycophancy-isnt-just-a-quirk-experts-consider-it-a-dark-pattern-to-turn-users-into-profit/) as "a deceptive design choice that manipulates users for profit." Keane compared the effect to addictive interface design, telling [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2025/08/25/ai-sycophancy-isnt-just-a-quirk-experts-consider-it-a-dark-pattern-to-turn-users-into-profit/): "It's a strategy to produce this addictive behavior, like infinite scrolling."

The CDT study lands amid broader scrutiny of how AI chatbots influence users. Lawmakers have separately moved to regulate companion chatbots, as [previously reported](/article/2026-05/26-senate-judiciary-committee-unanimously-advances-guard-act-to-ban-ai-companion-chatbots-for-minors).

## What We Don't Know

404 Media's coverage does not enumerate all 37 cataloged patterns or provide a full per-product breakdown of which interfaces use which tactics. The study presents a taxonomy intended to inform design rather than a regulatory finding, and it does not by itself establish legal liability for any named company. The companies behind the chatbots named in the study were not quoted in the available coverage.
