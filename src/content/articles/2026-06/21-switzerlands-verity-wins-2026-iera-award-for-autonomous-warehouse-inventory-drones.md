## Overview

Verity, the Zurich-based maker of self-flying warehouse inventory drones, has received the 2026 Award for Innovation and Entrepreneurship in Robotics & Automation (IERA), an annual prize from the International Federation of Robotics that recognizes companies turning robotics research into commercial products. The award, announced on June 16, 2026, lands as Verity reports installations across 13 countries, a scale that according to [The Robot Report](https://www.therobotreport.com/verity-adds-qualcomm-to-series-b-round-bringing-total-round-to-43m/) makes it a market leader in autonomous inventory management.

## What We Know

Verity builds drones that fly through warehouse and store aisles on their own, photographing shelves and reading barcodes to keep inventory records current. According to the [International Federation of Robotics announcement](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20260616141409/en/IERA-Award-2026-Goes-to-Flying-Warehouse-Robots-by-Verity-from-Switzerland), the aerial robots track inventory without GPS or human intervention, navigating aisles and scanning barcodes before returning to charging stations. The same announcement says Verity's systems have been deployed in about 200 warehouses worldwide, that the drones capture roughly 500,000 images per day, and that they operate for months at a time, needing only two to three battery replacements a year.

The company describes its product as asset-light and fast to deploy. Verity combines its autonomous drone technology with data analytics to give clients real-time visibility into their inventory, and says the system can be installed in one week, [The Robot Report](https://www.therobotreport.com/verity-adds-qualcomm-to-series-b-round-bringing-total-round-to-43m/) reported. That report also noted that Verity has installations across 13 countries and serves clients spanning retailers, third-party logistics firms, and manufacturers.

Verity's founder and CEO is Raffaello D'Andrea, and the firm was founded in 2014, according to [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2023/03/15/verity-raises-32m-as-ikea-stores-deploy-its-inventory-drones/). Its best-known customer is IKEA: the furniture retailer began implementing the technology in 2021 in Verity's native Switzerland and, as of 2023, had 100 of Verity's drones operating across 16 locations, [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2023/03/15/verity-raises-32m-as-ikea-stores-deploy-its-inventory-drones/) reported. Verity also lists logistics firm DSV and Samsung's SDS unit as clients, per the same report. IKEA has since continued expanding the fleet, [TechCrunch reported in 2024](https://techcrunch.com/2024/08/17/ikea-expands-its-inventory-drone-fleet/), noting that the scanning drones provide around-the-clock inventory updates and can "reach heights humans and most robots can't."

The technical challenge Verity solved is indoor autonomy. Warehouses lack the GPS signal that outdoor drones rely on for positioning, so the company built what [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2023/07/11/verity-brings-in-another-11m-for-its-inventory-drones/) described as systems with limited connectivity that let the drones "fly indoors and avoid collisions." Drones are well suited to inventory work because they are "small, nimble and can hit even the highest shelves," the same report noted.

The IERA recognition follows a sustained fundraising run. Verity raised an $18 million Series A in 2018 and a $32 million Series B in early 2023 led by A.P. Moller Holding, then extended that round by $11 million later in 2023 to bring it to $43 million, with Qualcomm Ventures joining as a new backer, according to [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2023/07/11/verity-brings-in-another-11m-for-its-inventory-drones/). "Verity's self-flying drone technology and advanced warehouse inventory analytics are helping transform supply chains by enabling end-to-end, real-time visibility," said Boaz Peer of Qualcomm Ventures in the same report.

## What We Don't Know

The International Federation of Robotics announcement is the primary public source for the award itself and for several operational figures, including the roughly 200-warehouse deployment count and the 500,000-images-per-day throughput; those specific numbers have not been independently corroborated by other outlets reviewed here. The announcement did not disclose updated revenue, headcount, or current customer totals beyond the deployment figures, and it did not detail how the award factors into Verity's commercial roadmap. The country-by-country breakdown of IKEA store deployments has been reported inconsistently across coverage and is not asserted here.

## Analysis

The IERA Award places Verity within a fast-growing field of warehouse automation, where flying robots are emerging as a complement to the ground-based picking and sorting systems that dominate logistics floors. The pitch is straightforward: drones reach high shelves that humans and wheeled robots struggle with, and they can run inventory counts continuously rather than during periodic manual audits. The recognition signals that, after years of pilots and funding rounds, indoor inventory drones are being judged by an industry body on the strength of a shipped, deployed product rather than a demo.
