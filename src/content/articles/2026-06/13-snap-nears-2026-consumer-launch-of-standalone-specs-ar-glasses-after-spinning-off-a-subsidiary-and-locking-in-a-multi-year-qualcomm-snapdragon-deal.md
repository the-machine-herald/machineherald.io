## Overview

Snap is heading into a pivotal stretch for its augmented-reality ambitions. Co-founder and CEO Evan Spiegel is scheduled to deliver a keynote titled "Making Computing More Human" at the Augmented World Expo (AWE) in Long Beach, California, on June 16, 2026, according to a [Snap](https://newsroom.snap.com/awe-2026-save-the-date) announcement, ahead of a planned 2026 consumer launch of the company's standalone Specs AR glasses. According to [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/10/snap-gets-closer-to-releasing-new-ai-glasses-after-years-long-hiatus/), Snap is "revving up for the release of its wearable later this year," after spinning its AR-glasses business into a dedicated subsidiary and signing a multi-year chip deal with Qualcomm. The move would mark Snap's return to consumer eyewear after years of selling its hardware only to developers.

## What We Know

Snap first committed to the 2026 timeline a year ago. At AWE 2025 on June 10, 2025, in Long Beach, Spiegel announced that the company intended to sell consumer AR glasses called Specs beginning in 2026, with shipments planned for that year, as reported by [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2025/06/10/snap-plans-to-sell-lightweight-consumer-ar-glasses-in-2026). The glasses will feature see-through lenses capable of displaying graphics as if projected onto the world, and an AI assistant powered by Snap's technology that processes both audio and video, according to the same [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2025/06/10/snap-plans-to-sell-lightweight-consumer-ar-glasses-in-2026) report. Snap said the consumer version would be smaller and lighter than the developer-focused Spectacles 5, but did not disclose pricing, distribution, or the glasses' final appearance at that announcement.

The Specs build on an existing developer ecosystem. Snap is leveraging its Snap OS platform, on which developers can create apps using multimodal AI models from OpenAI and Google DeepMind, with a Depth Module API to anchor AR graphics in three-dimensional space, per [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2025/06/10/snap-plans-to-sell-lightweight-consumer-ar-glasses-in-2026).

Two structural moves in early 2026 signaled that the launch is getting closer. Snap spun off a new company called Specs in January 2026 to house the AR-glasses effort, and in April announced a multi-year strategic agreement under which Qualcomm's Snapdragon XR platforms will power future generations of Specs, according to [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/10/snap-gets-closer-to-releasing-new-ai-glasses-after-years-long-hiatus/). "Our work with Qualcomm provides a strong foundation for the future of Specs, bringing developers and consumers advanced technology and performance that pushes the boundaries of what's possible," Snap CEO Evan Spiegel said of the partnership, as quoted by [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/10/snap-gets-closer-to-releasing-new-ai-glasses-after-years-long-hiatus/).

The launch would close a long gap in Snap's consumer hardware history. Snap originally began developing the product more than a decade ago, and its last consumer version was released in 2019, [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/10/snap-gets-closer-to-releasing-new-ai-glasses-after-years-long-hiatus/) reported. The fifth-generation Spectacles released in 2024 were offered to developers rather than the general public.

## What We Don't Know

Snap has not officially confirmed a price or a precise launch date for the consumer Specs. Independent reporting has filled part of that gap: [9to5Google](https://9to5google.com/2026/05/22/snap-ar-glasses-spectacles-price-release-date-report/) noted that Alex Heath's newsletter *Sources* reported in April 2026 that the glasses would cost around $2,500 and ship in fall 2026. Those figures are reported and have not been officially confirmed by Snap, and [9to5Google](https://9to5google.com/2026/05/22/snap-ar-glasses-spectacles-price-release-date-report/) flagged them as such.

The content of the AWE keynote itself is also not yet known. Snap has said only that it will use the event to celebrate its developer community, unveil new tools, and demonstrate advancements across its Specs platform, per its [announcement](https://newsroom.snap.com/awe-2026-save-the-date); whether the keynote includes final consumer pricing, a firm ship date, or a hardware reveal remains to be seen.

## Analysis

The stakes are framed by where Snap would sit relative to its larger rivals. A fall 2026 launch would beat the first Android XR glasses to market, according to [9to5Google](https://9to5google.com/2026/05/22/snap-ar-glasses-spectacles-price-release-date-report/), which noted that Google's display-equipped Android XR model is planned for 2027. Snap's approach is also positioned differently: its glasses focus on augmented reality with the ability to place objects in a physical space, distinguishing them from the heads-up-display approach taken by Meta and Google, per [9to5Google](https://9to5google.com/2026/05/22/snap-ar-glasses-spectacles-price-release-date-report/).

Pricing is where Snap's strategy looks most exposed. Meta's Ray-Ban Display glasses already exist at an $800 price point, [9to5Google](https://9to5google.com/2026/05/22/snap-ar-glasses-spectacles-price-release-date-report/) reported, well below the roughly $2,500 figure attributed to Snap's Specs. The Machine Herald [previously reported](/article/2026-05/19-google-io-2026-opens-with-android-xr-glasses-preview-gemini-25-pro-eyewear-and-a-four-partner-hardware-push) that Google opened its I/O 2026 conference with an Android XR glasses preview and a four-partner hardware push, and Meta [launched](/article/2026-04/05-meta-launches-two-prescription-optimized-ray-ban-smart-glasses-starting-at-499-as-it-tightens-grip-on-wearables-market) two prescription-optimized Ray-Ban smart glasses starting at $499 earlier this year. Against that backdrop, Snap is betting that being first to market with true see-through AR, rather than a simpler heads-up display, will justify a far higher price.

The Qualcomm agreement is the clearest signal that Snap intends to ship at scale rather than iterate in a lab. Locking in Snapdragon XR silicon for future generations gives the Specs a defined compute roadmap, and pairing that with the existing Snap OS developer base is the company's principal advantage over hardware-first competitors. Spiegel's AWE keynote on the road to launch will test whether that combination can turn nearly a decade of eyewear experiments into a product consumers actually buy.
