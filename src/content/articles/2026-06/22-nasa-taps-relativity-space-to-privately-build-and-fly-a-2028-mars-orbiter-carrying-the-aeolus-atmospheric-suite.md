## Overview

NASA on June 17, 2026 announced a public-private partnership with Relativity Space to advance Mars science, with the agency supplying a four-instrument atmospheric payload called Aeolus while the company builds the spacecraft and launches it toward the Red Planet, according to [NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-announces-public-private-partnership-to-advance-mars-science/). The mission is scheduled to launch in 2028 and is structured under what NASA describes as its first six-year reimbursable Space Act Agreement.

NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman announced the partnership, framing it as a way to stretch the agency's science budget. "Public-private partnerships like this are a force multiplier for science," he said, according to [NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-announces-public-private-partnership-to-advance-mars-science/). "By pairing NASA's world‑class instruments with commercial innovation and investment, we can deliver more science, more often, and reduce the time it takes to get essential data into the hands of researchers preparing for future human missions to Mars."

## What We Know

Under the arrangement, NASA's Ames Research Center in Silicon Valley, California, will design, build, and integrate the payload, while Relativity Space supplies the spacecraft, rocket, and cruise operations and manages mission operations, according to [NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-announces-public-private-partnership-to-advance-mars-science/). Reporting by [ExecutiveGov](https://www.executivegov.com/articles/nasa-relativity-space-aeolus-atmospheric-science-mars-mission) adds that the agency committed to support Aeolus instrument operations for one Martian year, a span echoed by [Universe Magazine](https://universemagazine.com/en/nasa-brings-in-a-private-company-for-a-scientific-mission-to-mars/), which reported NASA will fund and maintain the instruments for at least one Martian year.

Aeolus comprises four instruments, according to [NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-announces-public-private-partnership-to-advance-mars-science/): a Doppler Wind and Temperature Sounder (DWTS-Ozone), a Thermal Limb Sounder (TLS), a Surface Radiometric Sensor Package (SuRSeP), and a Wide-Field Context Camera (WFCC). Together, the suite is built to study temperature, dust, wind, and cloud conditions on the Red Planet, according to [Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/ex-google-ceo-eric-schmidts-relativity-space-selected-for-upcoming-nasa-mars-orbiter-mission/), which reported that the goal of the mission is to gather data that could one day help reduce risk to landings on Mars, both crewed and uncrewed.

The deal is notable for the launch provider. Relativity Space, led by former Google chief executive Eric Schmidt since March 2025, plans to fly the orbiter on its two-stage, reusable Terran R rocket, whose debut voyage is targeted for late 2026, according to [Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/ex-google-ceo-eric-schmidts-relativity-space-selected-for-upcoming-nasa-mars-orbiter-mission/). The company's rockets have not yet reached orbit. As [Universe Magazine](https://universemagazine.com/en/nasa-brings-in-a-private-company-for-a-scientific-mission-to-mars/) reported, Relativity's only launch took place in March 2023, when its Terran 1 rocket passed through the region of maximum aerodynamic pressure but the second-stage engine shut down and the vehicle did not reach orbit.

The Aeolus partnership comes as NASA winds down an earlier generation of Mars atmospheric science. The agency recently declared its MAVEN orbiter dead after 11 years, [as previously reported](/article/2026-06/09-nasa-declares-maven-mars-orbiter-dead-after-11-years-ending-the-mission-that-charted-how-mars-lost-its-atmosphere).

## What We Don't Know

NASA's announcement did not explicitly identify a private funding source for the mission, and [Universe Magazine](https://universemagazine.com/en/nasa-brings-in-a-private-company-for-a-scientific-mission-to-mars/) and [Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/ex-google-ceo-eric-schmidts-relativity-space-selected-for-upcoming-nasa-mars-orbiter-mission/) did not name a specific philanthropic or private backer beyond NASA's instrument commitment. The mission also hinges on a vehicle that has yet to fly: the Terran R's first flight is not expected until late 2026, leaving roughly two years between its debut and the 2028 Mars launch window, according to [Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/ex-google-ceo-eric-schmidts-relativity-space-selected-for-upcoming-nasa-mars-orbiter-mission/).
