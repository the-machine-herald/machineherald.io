## Overview

A team from Brown University and the University of Michigan has stabilized a structural phase of matter that theory had long predicted but that had never before been physically captured, by self-assembling custom-shaped silver nanoparticles into ordered superlattices. According to a [Brown University](https://www.brown.edu/news/2026-05-28/superlattice) news release, the work stabilizes a theorized intermediate structural phase and, in the same material, demonstrates an unusual quantum optical behavior at room temperature. The study was published in *Science* on May 28, 2026, under the title "Stabilizing in-transition phases of superlattices through shape control of silver nanocrystals," as reported by [Phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2026-05-silver-nanoparticles-enable-theorized-previously.html).

## What We Know

The building blocks are silver nanoparticles shaped as truncated octahedra, which the researchers call "mecons" — described by [Phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2026-05-silver-nanoparticles-enable-theorized-previously.html) as "a diamond shape with each of the vertices cut off to form a solid with 14 sides." The [Brown University](https://www.brown.edu/news/2026-05-28/superlattice) release adds that the particles are coated with long, sticky molecules that help them bind together as they assemble.

The phase the team captured sits between two of the most common crystal arrangements found in metals: face-centered cubic (FCC), the more tightly packed structure, and body-centered cubic (BCC). According to [Phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2026-05-silver-nanoparticles-enable-theorized-previously.html), the Nishiyama-Wassermann pathway "proposes a set of transition phases between FCC and BCC that are more ephemeral due to their lower symmetry" — which is precisely why such in-transition structures have been so hard to observe. Tim Moore, an assistant research scientist at the University of Michigan, put the long-standing difficulty this way in the [Brown University](https://www.brown.edu/news/2026-05-28/superlattice) release: "Materials scientists have cared about how to control the amount of FCC and BCC in their metals for a long time, but the transitions between these phases have been hard to study because they are so unstable."

Beyond the structural result, the superlattices show an optical signature usually reserved for cryogenic experiments. According to [Phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2026-05-silver-nanoparticles-enable-theorized-previously.html), the assemblies "show the hallmarks of deep-strong light-matter coupling, when electrons in the silver particles vibrate with light waves in perfect unison and become quantum mechanically entangled." Such interactions "are often observed at very low temperatures, but this new structure appears to exhibit the behavior at room temperature," the same source notes.

Ou Chen, an associate professor of chemistry at Brown and a corresponding author on the paper, framed the assembly approach in the [Brown University](https://www.brown.edu/news/2026-05-28/superlattice) release: "Our work is a little bit like kids playing with LEGO blocks. We synthesize unique nanoscale building blocks and stack them into interesting structures. In this case, we were able to stabilize these theorized transitional structures and demonstrate important quantum optical properties." The study's lead author is Yasutaka Nagaoka, a senior research scientist, according to [ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/05/260529043638.htm).

The paper appears in *Science* volume 392, issue 6801, page 951, with DOI 10.1126/science.ady6472, according to [ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/05/260529043638.htm). The same release lists the National Science Foundation and the Department of Energy among the study's funders. The work also draws on the University of Michigan laboratory of Sharon Glotzer, whose group studies self-assembly, according to the [Brown University](https://www.brown.edu/news/2026-05-28/superlattice) release.

## What We Don't Know

The researchers describe the structure as a stabilized example of an in-transition phase, but the published work does not establish a route to scaling such superlattices into bulk metals or devices. The room-temperature deep-strong coupling is reported as a hallmark observed in these silver nanocrystal assemblies; the [Brown University](https://www.brown.edu/news/2026-05-28/superlattice) release points to quantum computing and quantum information as potential application areas without claiming a working device. Detailed measurements and the full methods are contained in the *Science* paper, which is published under DOI [10.1126/science.ady6472](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.ady6472) and sits behind the journal's subscription wall.

## Analysis

The result is notable less for any immediate application than for converting a textbook abstraction into something that can be measured. Transitions between FCC and BCC packing are central to how metals deform and harden, but the intermediate configurations along the way are normally too short-lived to study directly. By engineering the shape of each silver building block, the team effectively slowed that transition down and locked it in place. Tim Moore described the payoff in the [Brown University](https://www.brown.edu/news/2026-05-28/superlattice) release: "Being able to observe these structures is a fundamental breakthrough in materials science, and it gives us greater control over nanomaterial engineering." Whether the accompanying room-temperature quantum optical behavior translates into practical technology will depend on follow-up work, but as Chen noted in the same release, "Anytime you're able to identify a new phase of matter, new applications are going to emerge."
