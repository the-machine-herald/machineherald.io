## Overview

Metal detectorists Rune Sætre and Vegard Sørlie were searching a field in eastern Norway on April 10 when their devices began turning up silver. They retrieved 19 coins before stopping and alerting archaeologists, [according to Archaeology Magazine](https://archaeology.org/news/2026/04/30/viking-coin-hoard-discovered-in-norway/). Within roughly a week, professionals working alongside the pair had recovered more than 3,000 silver coins from the same plot, [as Scientific American reported](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/spectacular-viking-coin-hoard-discovery-is-likely-the-largest-in-history/). The cache, since named the Mørstad hoard for the farm where it was found, is now the largest Viking Age coin discovery ever recorded in Norway.

## What We Know

The field sits near Rena, in the Østerdalen region of Innlandet County in eastern Norway, [per Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/spectacular-viking-coin-hoard-discovery-is-likely-the-largest-in-history/). The site is a working farm called Mørstad, which is also the source of the hoard's name, [as documented by Science Norway](https://www.sciencenorway.no/archaeology-culture-the-viking-age/norways-largest-viking-age-coin-hoard-has-been-discovered-truly-exceptional/2656877).

The coin count climbed quickly once professional excavation began. [Archaeology News reported](https://archaeologymag.com/2026/05/largest-viking-age-coin-hoard-in-norway/) more than 3,150 silver coins had been recovered by early May, with additional pieces still expected. [Smithsonian Magazine put the running total at 2,970](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smart-news/see-the-largest-viking-age-hoard-ever-found-in-norway-at-nearly-3000-coins-and-counting-the-cache-is-a-once-in-a-lifetime-find-180988657/) at the time of its May 4 dispatch and noted that the previous Norwegian record, the Arstad hoard, comprised 1,850 coins and was discovered in 1836.

The coins themselves span the late Viking Age. [Scientific American dates them from the 980s to the 1040s C.E.](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/spectacular-viking-coin-hoard-discovery-is-likely-the-largest-in-history/) and reports that they originate from England, Germany, Denmark, and Norway. [Archaeology News identifies coins struck for Cnut the Great, Æthelred II, Otto III, and Harald Hardrada](https://archaeologymag.com/2026/05/largest-viking-age-coin-hoard-in-norway/) among the recovered pieces. Norwegian-minted coins among the collection are linked to the period after Harald Hardrada returned from Byzantium around 1045, [as Arkeonews notes](https://arkeonews.net/unprecedented-find-of-more-than-3000-coins-becomes-norways-largest-viking-age-hoard/), since national minting in Norway only began under his reign.

[Archaeology Magazine reports](https://archaeology.org/news/2026/04/30/viking-coin-hoard-discovered-in-norway/) the burial likely dates to around 1050, near the close of the Viking Age. The same source records that the coins were probably stored in a leather pouch that decayed underground, leaving the coins to be scattered by centuries of plowing, and that despite this they "almost look newly minted."

## Who Is Involved

May-Tove Smiseth, an archaeologist with Innlandet County Municipality who handles metal detector finds for the region, is overseeing the field work. She told [Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/spectacular-viking-coin-hoard-discovery-is-likely-the-largest-in-history/) the discovery was "a truly unique discovery of the kind one might only experience once in an entire career." Speaking about how the detectorists handled the find, she told [Archaeology Magazine](https://archaeology.org/news/2026/04/30/viking-coin-hoard-discovered-in-norway/), "They have done a fantastic job and followed the regulations to the letter."

Numismatic analysis is being led by Svein Harald Gullbekk, a coin specialist at the Museum of Cultural History in Oslo. [Science Norway](https://www.sciencenorway.no/archaeology-culture-the-viking-age/norways-largest-viking-age-coin-hoard-has-been-discovered-truly-exceptional/2656877) quoted Gullbekk describing the find as "extraordinary" and "truly exceptional." He told [Archaeology Magazine](https://archaeology.org/news/2026/04/30/viking-coin-hoard-discovered-in-norway/), "So not only is the discovery itself extraordinary, but it's also connected to a very interesting period of Norwegian history."

Norway's Minister of Climate and Environment, Andreas Bjelland Eriksen, weighed in on the political stage, [telling Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/spectacular-viking-coin-hoard-discovery-is-likely-the-largest-in-history/), "This is a historic find. The fact that it is also from the Viking Age makes it even more spectacular." Jostein Bergstøl, an archaeologist at the Museum of Cultural History, is also involved with the excavation, [according to Smithsonian Magazine](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smart-news/see-the-largest-viking-age-hoard-ever-found-in-norway-at-nearly-3000-coins-and-counting-the-cache-is-a-once-in-a-lifetime-find-180988657/).

## What We Don't Know

The excavation is ongoing and the final coin count is unsettled. [Archaeology News](https://archaeologymag.com/2026/05/largest-viking-age-coin-hoard-in-norway/) and [Smithsonian Magazine](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smart-news/see-the-largest-viking-age-hoard-ever-found-in-norway-at-nearly-3000-coins-and-counting-the-cache-is-a-once-in-a-lifetime-find-180988657/) both note that more coins are expected as work continues at the site.

Who originally buried the silver, and why, is also unresolved. The dating places the burial roughly at 1050, around the time the Norwegian state began issuing its own coinage under Harald Hardrada, but no source identifies the owner or the circumstances of the deposit. Detailed cataloguing of which mints, kings, and proportions are represented in the hoard is still pending: numismatic specialists at the Museum of Cultural History's Coin Cabinet are continuing the work of identifying individual pieces, [as Archaeology News describes](https://archaeologymag.com/2026/05/largest-viking-age-coin-hoard-in-norway/).

## Context

Viking-age silver hoards are not unusual in Scandinavia, but a find this size in Norway is. [Smithsonian Magazine](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smart-news/see-the-largest-viking-age-hoard-ever-found-in-norway-at-nearly-3000-coins-and-counting-the-cache-is-a-once-in-a-lifetime-find-180988657/) reports that the Arstad hoard, found in 1836 with roughly 1,850 coins, had held the national record for nearly 200 years. [Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/spectacular-viking-coin-hoard-discovery-is-likely-the-largest-in-history/) notes that the last comparable Viking-era coin discovery in Norway occurred in 1950.

The coins are now being processed at the Museum of Cultural History in Oslo, [per Archaeology Magazine](https://archaeology.org/news/2026/04/30/viking-coin-hoard-discovered-in-norway/), where the cataloguing and conservation work will continue alongside any further finds at Mørstad.
