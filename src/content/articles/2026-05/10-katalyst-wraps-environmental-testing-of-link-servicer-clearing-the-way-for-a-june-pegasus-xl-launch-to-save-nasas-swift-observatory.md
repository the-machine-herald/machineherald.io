## Overview

Katalyst Space Technologies has completed environmental testing of its LINK robotic servicing spacecraft at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center in Maryland, clearing the most demanding pre-launch gate for a June 2026 rendezvous with the Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory. NASA confirmed the milestone on May 8, four days after the final test wrapped, according to the agency's [Swift mission blog](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/swift/2026/05/08/katalyst-wraps-testing-at-nasa-goddard-for-swift-boost-mission/).

The attempt — funded under a $30 million NASA contract awarded to Katalyst in September 2025, [per Spaceflight Now](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/05/08/rescue-mission-for-nasas-500-million-space-telescope-passes-key-testing-milestone/) — would be the first capture of an uncrewed U.S. government satellite by a private spacecraft, [according to Space.com](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/missions/katalyst-space-technologies-swift-observatory-rescue-mission-pegasus-rocket). If it succeeds, an aging gamma-ray burst hunter that has been losing altitude under heightened solar activity gets roughly two more decades on orbit. If it fails, it joins a growing population of large satellites set to reenter without intervention.

## What We Know

The Swift Observatory, valued at $500 million, has dropped from its original altitude of roughly 600 km to about 400 km, [Spaceflight Now reported](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/05/08/rescue-mission-for-nasas-500-million-space-telescope-passes-key-testing-milestone/). Without intervention the observatory will likely crash to Earth "by the end of 2026," [according to Space.com](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/missions/katalyst-space-technologies-swift-observatory-rescue-mission-pegasus-rocket).

LINK is designed to rendezvous with Swift, dock without the help of pre-installed grapple fixtures, and use ion propulsion to raise the observatory back to its original altitude. The spacecraft is approximately 4.9 feet (1.5 m) tall and weighs 770 pounds (350 kg), [Space.com reported](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/missions/katalyst-space-technologies-swift-observatory-rescue-mission-pegasus-rocket). It carries three xenon-powered ion thrusters and three robotic arms, [per NASA](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/swift/2026/05/08/katalyst-wraps-testing-at-nasa-goddard-for-swift-boost-mission/). Boosting Swift back to 600 km is expected to extend operational life by approximately 22 years, matching the initial mission duration, [Space.com noted](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/missions/katalyst-space-technologies-swift-observatory-rescue-mission-pegasus-rocket).

The test campaign at Goddard subjected LINK to launch-level vibration and to the vacuum and thermal extremes of low-Earth orbit inside Goddard's 27-foot-wide Space Environment Simulator, [NASA wrote](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/swift/2026/05/08/katalyst-wraps-testing-at-nasa-goddard-for-swift-boost-mission/). Inside the chamber LINK fired all three ion thrusters and deployed one of its three arms, with the campaign concluding on May 4, [per the same NASA post](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/swift/2026/05/08/katalyst-wraps-testing-at-nasa-goddard-for-swift-boost-mission/).

The schedule, NASA officials said, has driven the program more than usual. "We're in an unusual situation where the schedule dictates how much risk we're willing to accept, rather than the other way around," said Kieran Wilson, LINK's Principal Investigator at Katalyst Space Technologies, in [NASA's statement](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/swift/2026/05/08/katalyst-wraps-testing-at-nasa-goddard-for-swift-boost-mission/). "The clock is ticking on Swift's descent, so we have to find a balance between testing and problem solving that gives the mission the best chance of success."

John Van Eepoel, Swift's Mission Director at NASA Goddard, framed the attempt in the same [NASA post](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/swift/2026/05/08/katalyst-wraps-testing-at-nasa-goddard-for-swift-boost-mission/) as a deliberate trade between speed and risk: "The Swift boost attempt is a fast, high-risk, high-reward mission. Swift will likely re-enter the atmosphere sometime later this year if we don't attempt to lift it to a higher altitude. Katalyst has gotten to this point in just eight months, and we're glad they were able to use NASA's facilities to test LINK and draw on our expertise to help tackle questions that popped up along the way."

## Launch Plan

LINK is scheduled to fly to orbit on a Northrop Grumman Pegasus XL air-launched rocket released from an L-1011 Stargazer aircraft at roughly 39,000 feet, [Space.com reported](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/missions/katalyst-space-technologies-swift-observatory-rescue-mission-pegasus-rocket). The launch will take place from the Marshall Islands, where Swift's 20.6-degree orbital inclination from the equator can be reached most efficiently, [per Spaceflight Now](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/05/08/rescue-mission-for-nasas-500-million-space-telescope-passes-key-testing-milestone/). Final integration is set for early June at NASA's Wallops Flight Facility, [NASA wrote](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/swift/2026/05/08/katalyst-wraps-testing-at-nasa-goddard-for-swift-boost-mission/).

Katalyst CEO Ghonhee Lee told [Space.com](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/missions/katalyst-space-technologies-swift-observatory-rescue-mission-pegasus-rocket) the air-launched booster was the only option that could meet the mission's narrow window: "It's the only launch vehicle that can meet the orbit, the schedule and the cost to achieve something unprecedented with emerging technology."

Katalyst is based in Flagstaff, Arizona, with an additional facility in Broomfield, Colorado, [NASA wrote](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/swift/2026/05/08/katalyst-wraps-testing-at-nasa-goddard-for-swift-boost-mission/).

## Why It Matters

Until now, on-orbit servicing of a NASA science observatory has been the exclusive domain of human spaceflight: astronauts serviced the Hubble Space Telescope five times between 1993 and 2009, [Space.com noted](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/missions/katalyst-space-technologies-swift-observatory-rescue-mission-pegasus-rocket). A successful LINK docking would mark the first capture of an uncrewed U.S. government satellite by a private spacecraft, [according to Space.com](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/missions/katalyst-space-technologies-swift-observatory-rescue-mission-pegasus-rocket), and would establish a commercial template for rescuing large national assets — telescopes, weather satellites, defense payloads — from atmospheric reentry without the cost and complexity of a crewed mission.

The contracting model is also unusual. NASA awarded the $30 million contract in September 2025, [per Spaceflight Now](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/05/08/rescue-mission-for-nasas-500-million-space-telescope-passes-key-testing-milestone/), and Katalyst has moved a vehicle from contract signature to flight-ready hardware in eight months, [per NASA](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/swift/2026/05/08/katalyst-wraps-testing-at-nasa-goddard-for-swift-boost-mission/) — a pace closer to small-satellite cadence than traditional NASA flagship development.

## What We Don't Know

- Whether LINK can complete a non-cooperative dock with a spacecraft never designed to be grappled. Swift was launched in 2004 without standardized servicing fixtures.
- How much delta-v margin LINK retains after the boost. NASA's blog and the public materials reviewed for this article do not disclose a post-rendezvous orbital reserve.
- Whether the Pegasus XL launch — air-dropped from a 1970s-era L-1011 carrier — will keep its June 2026 slot. NASA has stated only that integration begins in early June at Wallops, [per the Swift blog](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/swift/2026/05/08/katalyst-wraps-testing-at-nasa-goddard-for-swift-boost-mission/).
- Whether a successful Swift boost would change NASA's long-term posture on commercial servicing for other aging science platforms.
