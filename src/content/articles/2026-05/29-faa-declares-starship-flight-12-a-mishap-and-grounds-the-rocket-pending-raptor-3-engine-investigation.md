## Overview

The Federal Aviation Administration declared SpaceX's Starship Flight 12 a mishap on May 27, 2026, five days after the rocket's first version 3 test flight, and ordered the company to complete a supervised investigation before the vehicle can fly again. The agency's formal classification halts Starship operations at a critical moment for SpaceX and for NASA's Artemis lunar program, which depends on the vehicle to land astronauts on the Moon.

## What Happened on May 22

Starship Flight 12 lifted off at 6:33 p.m. ET on May 22, 2026 from Launch Pad 2 at Starbase, Texas — the second attempt after the mission was [scrubbed the previous day](/article/2026-05/22-spacex-scrubs-first-starship-v3-launch-after-hydraulic-failure-sets-may-22-window-for-flight-12) due to a hydraulic pin failure in the launch tower's quick disconnect arm, [according to SpaceQ Media](https://spaceq.ca/starship-flight-12-validates-v3-reentry-systems-despite-early-booster-termination/).

The mission marked the inaugural flight of Starship version 3, featuring newly introduced Raptor 3 engines with a redesigned ignition system, increased thrust capability, and a lighter profile achieved by internally integrating sensors and controllers, [as SpaceQ Media reported](https://spaceq.ca/starship-flight-12-validates-v3-reentry-systems-despite-early-booster-termination/). All 33 booster engines ignited successfully at liftoff.

One minute and 42 seconds into flight, one engine on Super Heavy Booster 19 shut down; the vehicle continued on its ascent profile, [according to TechTimes](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/317326/20260528/spacex-starship-grounded-after-flight-12-booster-failure-faa-investigation-threatens-ipo-timeline.htm). After stage separation, however, the booster encountered a more severe problem. SpaceX reported the vehicle "was unable to light all planned engines and performed a partial boostback burn that ended early," as noted by [TechTimes](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/317326/20260528/spacex-starship-grounded-after-flight-12-booster-failure-faa-investigation-threatens-ipo-timeline.htm). The planned boostback burn, which should have lasted approximately one minute, terminated in less than 20 seconds, [according to SpaceNews](https://spacenews.com/faa-requires-mishap-investigation-into-latest-starship-launch/). Booster 19 subsequently plummeted into the Gulf of America at roughly 1,500 km/h, [SpaceNews reported](https://spacenews.com/faa-requires-mishap-investigation-into-latest-starship-launch/).

During the broadcast, SpaceX communications team member Dan Huot noted: "We are not seeing as many booster engines ignited as we expected for boostback, but we are seeing six good engines on ship," [according to TechTimes](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/317326/20260528/spacex-starship-grounded-after-flight-12-booster-failure-faa-investigation-threatens-ipo-timeline.htm).

## Ship 39 Succeeds

Despite the booster anomaly, Ship 39 — the Starship upper stage — performed well. The vehicle lost one of its three Raptor Vacuum engines but the flight computer compensated by gimballing the remaining engines and extending their burn time, [SpaceQ Media reported](https://spaceq.ca/starship-flight-12-validates-v3-reentry-systems-despite-early-booster-termination/). Ship 39 successfully reached its planned sub-orbital trajectory, survived atmospheric reentry, and achieved a controlled splashdown in the Indian Ocean west of Australia one hour and six minutes after launch, [according to Teslarati](https://www.teslarati.com/spacex-starship-v3-flight-12/).

SpaceX deployed 22 objects during the mission, including 20 Starlink simulator satellites and two camera-equipped satellites designed to scan Starship's heat shield from orbit — a capability intended to accelerate between-flight inspections, [Teslarati reported](https://www.teslarati.com/spacex-starship-v3-flight-12/).

Elon Musk celebrated the outcome on social media: "Congratulations @SpaceX team on an epic first Starship V3 launch and landing! You scored a goal for humanity," [per Teslarati](https://www.teslarati.com/spacex-starship-v3-flight-12/).

## The FAA Mishap Determination

The FAA defines a mishap as including "the failure to complete a launch or reentry as planned," [according to SpaceNews](https://spacenews.com/faa-requires-mishap-investigation-into-latest-starship-launch/). Under 14 CFR Part 450 regulations, the agency must determine root causes and approve corrective actions before authorizing additional flights, [as TechTimes noted](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/317326/20260528/spacex-starship-grounded-after-flight-12-booster-failure-faa-investigation-threatens-ipo-timeline.htm).

On May 27, the FAA issued its formal determination: "After a thorough assessment of the operation, the FAA has determined the May 22 SpaceX Starship Flight 12 launch resulted in a mishap. The mishap involved the Super Heavy booster as it flew back to the Gulf of America after stage separation," [as reported by SpaceNews](https://spacenews.com/faa-requires-mishap-investigation-into-latest-starship-launch/).

The agency confirmed that the booster's debris fell within an FAA-activated debris response zone and that several aircraft experienced departure delays, though no public injuries or property damage occurred, [SpaceNews reported](https://spacenews.com/faa-requires-mishap-investigation-into-latest-starship-launch/).

The FAA stated it will "oversee the SpaceX-led investigation, be involved in every step of the process, and approve SpaceX's final report," [according to Spaceflight Now](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/05/27/faa-requires-spacex-led-mishap-investigation-before-resumption-of-starship-launches/). SpaceX declined to comment when contacted, [per TechTimes](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/317326/20260528/spacex-starship-grounded-after-flight-12-booster-failure-faa-investigation-threatens-ipo-timeline.htm).

## What Comes Next

SpaceX has hardware ready for the next mission — Ship 40 and Booster 20 — but will likely forego orbital operations and tower catches pending investigation results, [Spaceflight Now reported](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/05/27/faa-requires-spacex-led-mishap-investigation-before-resumption-of-starship-launches/).

Investigation timelines based on historical precedent are measured in weeks to months. Blue Origin's New Glenn mishap was declared complete within approximately 33 days, [SpaceNews noted](https://spacenews.com/faa-requires-mishap-investigation-into-latest-starship-launch/). NASASpaceFlight estimates a July-August 2026 window for Starship Flight 13, [according to TechTimes](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/317326/20260528/spacex-starship-grounded-after-flight-12-booster-failure-faa-investigation-threatens-ipo-timeline.htm).

The grounding arrives at a consequential moment for SpaceX beyond the launch program itself. The company's IPO roadshow was scheduled for June 4, with trading potentially beginning June 12, 2026, making a pre-IPO Starship flight "unlikely based on current timelines," [TechTimes reported](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/317326/20260528/spacex-starship-grounded-after-flight-12-booster-failure-faa-investigation-threatens-ipo-timeline.htm).

For NASA, the investigation delays demonstrations required for Artemis IV lunar landing missions targeted for 2028, including orbital propellant transfer and an uncrewed lunar landing capability, [as TechTimes reported](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/317326/20260528/spacex-starship-grounded-after-flight-12-booster-failure-faa-investigation-threatens-ipo-timeline.htm).

Flight 12 is SpaceX's eighth mishap investigation across nine Starship test vehicles, [according to Spaceflight Now](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/05/27/faa-requires-spacex-led-mishap-investigation-before-resumption-of-starship-launches/). The investigation will focus specifically on the Raptor 3 engines, which made their program debut on this flight — introducing a new variable into an already complex engine failure analysis.

## What We Don't Know

The FAA has not announced a target completion date for the investigation. Whether the Raptor 3 engine failures on both the booster and upper stage during the same flight share a common cause remains to be determined. SpaceX has not disclosed technical details about the engine anomalies beyond its broadcast acknowledgment that fewer boostback engines ignited than planned.
